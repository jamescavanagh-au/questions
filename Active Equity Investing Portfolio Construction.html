Question 3

Discuss two concerns regarding the effect of the new contributions on the ability of Voyager’s current strategy to outperform its benchmark.

General Feedback

One concern is the size of the new contributions relative to the market capitalizations of the current portfolio holdings. The total market capitalization of the three stocks currently held in the portfolio is $800 million, which is significantly less than the total new contributions of $1.1 billion. Hypothetically, Voyager’s portfolio could acquire 100% of Company A, Company B, and Company C and still have a large amount of uninvested cash.

Another concern is the average daily trading volume of the current portfolio holdings relative to their total shares outstanding. Stock A, Stock B, and Stock C each have less than 0.1% of their total shares outstanding traded daily. Hence, the potential price movement and trading costs of purchasing a large number of shares of the current constituents could be high and could negatively erode Voyager’s alpha.

Question 4

Contrast a top-down, systematic stock selection approach and Voyager’s current approach.

General Feedback

A top-down, systematic approach emphasizes macro factors and factor timing and typically results in a diversified portfolio. A bottom-up, discretionary approach emphasizes firm-specific characteristics and may incorporate factor timing. The resulting portfolio may be concentrated or diversified depending on the manager’s style and strategy.

Question 5

Discuss one difference between the investment strategies of AE and CC that can be inferred from the data in Exhibit 1.

General Feedback

AE has a higher active share than CC (0.92 vs. 0.71) and a higher active risk than CC (15.1% vs. 7.2%). As such, AE’s portfolio holdings are more differentiated relative to the benchmark than CC’s holdings are, and AE’s returns are less correlated with the benchmark than CC’s returns are (note that the benchmark in this case is the Russell 1000® Index). Exhibit 1 also shows that AE has fewer portfolio holdings than CC (42 vs. 124), which may explain why it has both higher active share and higher active risk.

Overall, AE has high security concentration and high idiosyncratic risk. Given the somewhat lower R-squared (0.72), factor exposures do not appear to have as strong an influence. Given these considerations, AE appears to be using a concentrated stock-picking strategy. On the other hand, CC has a moderate level of security concentration and a moderate level of idiosyncratic risk. It has a much higher R-squared (0.98), which suggests that factor selection is very important. Therefore, CC appears to be using a concentrated factor-betting strategy.

Question 6

Calculate the number of days it would take Reed to purchase the maximum allowable position in the average small-cap company in her pipeline.

General Feedback

The number of days to purchase the maximum allowable position in the average small-cap company in Reed’s pipeline is calculated as follows:

Maximum position size = Strategy AUM × Position size limit = $2 billion × 2% = $40 million

Average daily volume = Average market capitalization × Average percentage traded each day = $1 billion × 2% = $20 million

Daily purchase limit = Average daily volume × Daily volume limit = $20 million × 5% = $1 million

Number of days to purchase = Maximum position size / Daily purchase limit

= $40 million / $1 million = 40 days

Question 7

Discuss one way in which AE’s growth in AUM over the past five years has most likely impacted portfolio construction.

General Feedback

The data in Exhibit 2 show that, as AE’s AUM has increased over the past five years, the average number of portfolio holdings has more than doubled (from 18 to 40). We know that the number of small-cap names in the portfolio has increased, based both on Reed’s comments and the change in the beta to size during the period (from 0.05 to 0.20). As smaller-cap names typically take longer to purchase, this has likely contributed to the decline in portfolio turnover (from 40% to 20%). The implicit costs associated with purchasing small-cap names may be limiting Reed’s ability to generate alpha.

In addition, as the fund has gotten bigger, it appears that factor exposures have drifted. During the period, AE has increased its exposure to the market factor (from 0.84 to 0.96). The increase in the number of positions has likely reduced idiosyncratic risk and may be limiting Reed’s ability to generate alpha. In addition, AE has decreased its exposure to the Quality (from 0.3 to 0.0), Momentum (from 0.0 to –0.2), and Value (from 0.2 to 0.1) factors. As AUM has grown, Reed has likely had difficulty finding additional names that fit her strategy, which has resulted in a meaningful amount of style drift.

Question 10

Determine which manager has a higher expected active return. (Webb, Daniels) Justify your response.

General Feedback

According to the fundamental law of active management, expected active return equals:

E(RA) = IC√BRσRATC

where IC is the information coefficient, BR (breadth) is the number of independent investment decisions made each year, σRA is the manager’s active risk, and TC is the transfer coefficient.

Webb’s E(RA) = 0.25 × √225 × 3% × 0.40 = 4.50%

Daniels’s E(RA) = 0.30 × √100 × 5% × 0.60 = 9.00%

Daniels has a higher expected active return—twice that of Webb.

Question 11

Determine how many more independent investment decisions the lower-active-return manager would need to make to have the same expected active return as the higher-active-return manager.

General Feedback

In order to equal Daniels’s expected active return, Webb would need to double the effect of breadth: 2 × √225 = √4(225) = √900 independent decisions. If the required breadth is 900, Webb would need to make 900 – 225 = 675 more independent investment decisions than at present.
