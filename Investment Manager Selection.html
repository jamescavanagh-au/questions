VIGNETTE 3

Mat Dalton is the director of investments at Wood Valley University’s endowment fund. He has been instructed by the university’s Investment Committee to conduct a search for a new manager to replace an existing manager for the equity portion of the fund’s emerging markets mandate.

The manager to be replaced, ZA Investors, was hired four years ago and has been underperforming for the past two years. Although the poor performance of two years ago could be explained by the fund being underweighted in Asia and technologies, the poor performance of last year could not be easily explained.

Before starting his search, Dalton reviews the Investment Committee’s recent change to the manager selection policy. There is to be increased emphasis on the past record of performance, with a requirement that the manager has demonstrated an ability to earn excess returns over a longer period than that of the current policy. Dalton and his colleague Sujata Battha discuss the policy:

Dalton: I think the change lowers the chance that we will hire another future underperformer like ZA.

Battha: That might be true, but we will miss an opportunity from a manager using a newer thesis.

Dalton: We also need to track the future performance of managers that are fired. Our concern would be discovering that they outperform in subsequent periods.

As part of the selection process Dalton runs a screen using an external database and identifies three managers he wishes to examine more closely. He starts his analysis by looking at the investment philosophy and process of each manager and prepares a summary of key information he has gathered (Exhibit 1). He intends to rank the managers on key facets of the selection process where possible.

Exhibit 1 Comparison of Key Information for Three Potential Managers

Dalton decides to phone the funds to ask some specific questions. He asks the senior manager from Manager 2 about the recent change in investment strategy from growth to value. The manager replies that he exploits mispricings in emerging markets that arise because of their weak regulatory systems. He further notes that he had successfully implemented this strategy at his previous fund and is now training his current staff to follow this method.

Dalton finds out from Manager 1 that it offers both separately managed accounts (SMAs) and pooled vehicles. The manager suggests that its pooled vehicle will be better suited to Wood Valley’s needs.

Question 9: Which statement from the discussion between Dalton and Battha about the change in policy for manager selection most likely reflects a Type I error?

A. Battha’s

Incorrect. A Type II error is a false negative–the non-rejection of the null hypotheses that the manager is unskillful. It would arise from not hiring or firing a manager who subsequently outperforms or meets expectations. Battha’s comment about missed opportunities describes a Type II error.

B. Dalton’s first statement

Correct. A Type I error is a false positive–rejection of the null hypothesis that the manager is unskilled. In this case, it would be hiring or retaining a manager who subsequently underperforms expectations. Dalton’s first statement, about lowering the chance of hiring another future underperformer, describes a Type 1 error.

C. Dalton’s second statement

Incorrect. A Type II error is a false negative. Dalton’s second statement describes a Type II error, firing a manager who subsequently outperforms.

Question 10: Based on Exhibit 1, which of the managers most likely ranks highest in terms of the quality of its investment personnel?

A. Manager 1

Correct. The main characteristics in determining the quality of investment personnel are sufficient expertise and experience, sufficient depth to execute the strategy, exposure to key-person risk, and incentives to reduce personnel turnover. Manager 1 has the longest experience and uses a team approach to selecting investments, and therefore, it is not subject to key-person risk. It also has the lowest employee turnover and a consistent investment strategy, indicating experience.

B. Manager 2

Incorrect. Manager 2 suffers from key-person risk, with only one senior manager making the decisions. It has the highest employee turnover, and the manager has been at the fund for only one year. Therefore, Manager 2 has lower-quality investment personnel than Manager 1.

C. Manager 3

Incorrect. Manager 3 follows a global small-cap multi-strategy, which would require a large team to be effective. However, it has a team of only four. An even-numbered team of four may also make decision making difficult if there is not consensus. The staff turnover is higher than for Manger 1. Therefore, Manager 3 would rank lower than Manager 1 in terms of the quality of investment personnel.

Question 11: Based on his phone call with the senior manager from Manager 2, which of the following aspects of the fund’s investment philosophy should be of the greatest concern to Dalton?

A. The repeatability of the process

Correct. Investment philosophy refers to a manager’s assumptions about market efficiency and the factors that drive performance. The recent change in style from growth to value for Manager 2 should be a concern for Dalton when assessing investment philosophy. Even though the manager notes that he has successfully implemented his strategy in a previous fund, it is not clear whether he can replicate those results in his new position with different staff. The staff is not currently trained in his strategy, and it is also not known whether the strategy was directed at emerging markets.

B. The structural inefficiencies identified

Incorrect. As an active manager, Manager 2 is exploiting mispricings due to structural inefficiencies caused by regulatory systems, which may be long-lived, therefore making the strategy repeatable.

C. The behavioral inefficiencies identified

Incorrect. Behavioral inefficiencies are created by the actions of other market participants, and none were identified here.

Question 12: Which of the following reasons would least support Manager 1’s suggested choice of investment vehicle for Wood Valley?

A. Lower costs

Incorrect. Manager 1 suggests the use of a pooled vehicle, which has lower cost compared to an SMA. For a university endowment, keeping costs low would be an advantage and therefore supports the choice of a pooled vehicle.

B. Lower tracking risk

Incorrect. Manager 1 suggests the use of a pooled vehicle, which has reduced tracking risk compared to an SMA, so that reason supports the choice of a pooled vehicle.

C. Ability to customize the portfolio

Correct. Manager 1 suggests a pooled vehicle. The ability to customize a portfolio is an advantage of an SMA, not a pooled vehicle. Therefore, that reason does not support the choice of a pooled vehicle.

Jacob’s investment team identifies several potential managers based on qualitative factors, performance, and fee schedule. For additional due diligence to select and monitor potential managers, Jacob intends to use a type of style analysis that does not require a large amount of difficult-to-acquire data and is not subject to window dressing.

Jacob favors hiring Schwinn Capital and asks her team to calculate the manager’s net active return using the return and fee structure for the most recent year, as shown in Exhibit 1.

Exhibit 1

Before Jacob approves the hiring of Schwinn Capital, she wants to ensure they avoid preventable errors. Her team offers the following statements:

Statement 1: Type I errors are more transparent and create explicit costs.
Statement 2: Type II errors create opportunity costs and are psychologically less painful than Type I errors.
Statement 3: Type II errors are relatively easy to measure.
Question 13: Identify the behavioral bias Jacob exhibits when directing her team to invest in alternatives. (Groupthink bias, Authority bias, Aversion to complexity) Justify your response.

General Feedback

Jacob exhibits authority bias because she is in a position of authority and is making the decision independently. Authority bias involves groups deferring to a group member that is a subject matter expert or in a position of authority.

Groupthink bias is incorrect because investing in alternatives is an individual decision by Jacob, who is in a position of authority. Groupthink occurs when a team minimizes conflict and dissent in reaching and maintaining a consensus.

Aversion to complexity is incorrect because Jacob is not giving disproportionate attention to trivial issues at the expense of important but harder-to-grasp or contested topics.

Question 14: Determine the type of style analysis Jacob’s team should use for additional due diligence. (Holdings-based style analysis, Returns-based style analysis) Discuss one additional benefit of this approach.

General Feedback

Jacob should use a returns-based style analysis (RBSA) for additional due diligence to select and monitor potential managers. RBSA is a top-down approach that involves estimating a portfolio’s sensitivities to security market indexes representing a range of distinct factors. Advantages of RBSA are that it:

  • does not require a large amount of additional or difficult-to-acquire data.
  • can be estimated even for complicated strategies.
  • is not subject to window dressing.
  • is comparable across managers and through time.
Question 15: Calculate the net active return for Schwinn Capital.

General Feedback

Base fee = 0.25%

Return subject to sharing fee = Gross active return – Base fee = 3.50% – 0.25% = 3.25%

Additional fee due to sharing fee = Return subject to sharing fee × Sharing fee = 3.25% × 20% = 0.65%

Billed fee = Base fee + Additional fee due to sharing fee = 0.25% + 0.65% = 0.90%

Net active return = Gross active return – Billed fee = 3.50% – 0.90% = 2.60%

Question 16: Determine which of the team’s statements is most likely incorrect. (Statement 1, Statement 2, Statement 3) Justify your response.

General Feedback

Statement 1 is correct because Type I errors create explicit costs and are more transparent to investors. To the manager, they entail not only the regret of an incorrect decision but the pain of having to explain this decision to the investor.

Statement 2 is correct because Type II errors create opportunity costs and are less painful psychologically as individuals appear to put less weight on opportunity cost, which is also difficult to measure.

Statement 3 is incorrect because Type II errors are less likely to be measured. The performance impact of not selecting a particular manager is often not tracked and is difficult to measure.

Question 17: Which of the following qualitative considerations is most associated with determining whether investment manager selection will result in superior repeatable performance?

General Feedback

B is correct. A critical element of manager selection is to assess if the investment process is superior, repeatable, and can be consistently applied.

Question 18: The manager selection process begins by defining the universe of feasible managers. When defining this manager universe, the selection process should avoid:

General Feedback

A is correct. The focus of the initial screening process is on building a universe of managers that could potentially satisfy the identified portfolio need and should not focus on historical performance. This better aligns their interests with those of Connell.

Alternative 1

Question 21: Connell asks the adviser about the conditions under which any form of style analysis would be useful for understanding the funds he is considering.

Identify the conditions under which the adviser would find style analysis most useful.

General Feedback

The adviser would find style analysis most useful, whether it be returns-based (RBSA) or holdings-based (HBSA), when applied to strategies that hold publicly-traded securities where pricing is frequent. It can be applied to other strategies (hedge funds and private equity, for example), but the insights drawn from a style analysis of such strategies are more likely to be used for designing additional lines of inquiry in the course of due diligence rather than for confirmation of the investment process.

In addition, style analysis, whether returns-based or holdings-based, must be meaningful, accurate, consistent, and timely in order to be useful. Accordingly, style analysis would be most useful to Connell in understanding most of the asset classes in the funds he is considering, including the equities and bonds. However, it would be less meaningful for evaluating the venture capital assets since they are not traded and are thus illiquid.

VIGNETTE 6

Donna Grimmett is working with a financial adviser to establish her investment goals for €850,000, which she recently earned as a bonus. She asks the adviser about how to best select a manager for her funds.

The adviser responds that both qualitative and quantitative components are involved in outlining a framework for identifying, evaluating, and ultimately selecting a manager.

Question 22: Describe two considerations for each type of component recommended to Grimmett for her manager selection process.

General Feedback

Qualitative

Process and people: Evaluating the manager’s investment process, including the manager’s philosophy, process, people, and portfolio. This consideration is broadly described as part of “investment due diligence.”

Operational due diligence: Evaluating the manager’s infrastructure and firm, including the accuracy of the manager’s track record and whether the record fully reflects risks; the back office processes and procedures; the terms and if they are acceptable and appropriate for the strategy and vehicle; and the firm’s profitability, its culture, and if it’s likely to remain in business. This and the following bulleted considerations as a whole are broadly described as part of “operational due diligence”:

• Investment vehicle: Is the investment vehicle suitable for the portfolio need?

• Terms: Are the terms acceptable and appropriate for the strategy and vehicle?

• Monitoring: Does the manager continue to be the “best” fit for the portfolio need?

Quantitative:

Attribution and appraisal: To assess if the manager has displayed skill in investing.

The capture ratio: How has the manager performed in “up” versus “down” markets?

Drawdown: Does the return distribution exhibit large drawdowns?

Question 23: Grimmett asks the adviser if any other preparatory steps should be taken before choosing the best investment manager(s). The adviser produces a checklist related to manager selection in response to Grimmett’s question.

General Feedback

Grimmett, with her adviser, would ensure the following tasks were performed prior to manager selection:

• Decide that outside support is necessary.

• Complete an investment policy statement (IPS).

• Determine the appropriate asset allocation.

Short of these key actions, Grimmett will be unable to identify the managers who fit her needs, confirm that the managers are suitable for her IPS, and be confident that they will act upon the appropriate asset allocation.

Question 24: A decision-making investor is most likely to worry more about making a Type I error than a Type II error because:

General Feedback

B is correct. Type I errors are more easily measured than Type II errors. In addition, Type I errors may be linked to the compensation of the decision maker. Type I errors are errors of mistaken rejection, whereas Type II are errors failing to detect a true relationship. Firing a skilled manager is less transparent to the investor.

General Feedback

Type I:

Hiring Firm D, which later underperformed expectations, resulted in a Type I error. A Type I error occurs when a manager is hired or retained who subsequently underperforms expectations. This situation involves rejecting the null hypothesis of no skill when it is correct.

It is most likely Boinic will find its hiring of Firm D to be psychologically troubling.

Decision makers appear predisposed to worry more about Type I errors than Type II errors. Potential reasons for this focus are as follows:

  • Psychologically, people seek to avoid feelings of regret. Type I errors are errors of mistaken rejection, active decisions that turn out to be incorrect, whereas Type II errors are errors of failing to detect a true relationship. Type I errors create explicit costs, whereas Type II errors create opportunity costs. Because individuals appear to put less weight on opportunity costs, Type I errors are psychologically more painful than Type II errors.

  • Type I errors are more transparent to investors, so they entail not only the regret of an incorrect decision but the pain of having to explain this decision to the investor. Type II errors, such as firing (or not hiring) a manager with skill, are less transparent to investors—unless the investor tracks fired managers or evaluates the universe themselves.

Type II:

Not hiring Firm B, which later outperformed expectations, resulted in a Type II error. A Type II error occurs when a manager who subsequently outperforms, or performs in line with, expectations is not hired. This situation involves not rejecting an incorrect null hypothesis. As noted previously, a Type II error is typically less psychologically troubling than a Type I error.

The other three decisions did not result in either a Type I or Type II error.

General Feedback
