Asset size constraint: Access to asset classes such as private equity may be constrained for smaller asset owners like the Yangs, whose entire investment portfolio is worth only $500,000. Question 3: Determine which of the Yangs' statements best exhibits representativeness bias.

(Statement 1, Statement 2, Statement 3)

Justify your response.

General Feedback

Statement 3 best exhibits representativeness (or recency) bias. After recently reading in the newspaper that emerging market stocks have been constantly rising in value over the past few months, the Yangs are exhibiting representativeness, or recency, bias by wanting to invest in the emerging market stocks without further research. Representativeness, or recency, bias is the tendency to overweight the importance of the most recent observations and information relative to a longer-dated or more comprehensive set of long-term observations and information. Tactical shifts in asset allocation undertaken in response to recent returns or news are particularly susceptible to recency bias. Return chasing is a common manifestation of recency bias, and it results in overweighting asset classes with good recent performance.

Statement 1 reflects loss aversion. The Yangs are indifferent to the volatility of their portfolio gains but strongly prefer not to incur any losses, which is evidence of loss aversion bias. Loss-aversion bias is an emotional bias in which people tend to strongly prefer avoiding losses as opposed to achieving gains. The utility derived from a gain is much lower than the utility given up with an equivalent loss.

Statement 2 reflects illusion of control. By retaining a large, concentrated legacy asset that contributes diversifiable risk rather than diversifying their position in the stock of the company where Bill works, the Yangs display illusion of control bias. The illusion of control is a cognitive bias—the tendency to overestimate one’s ability to control events. It can be exacerbated by overconfidence, an emotional bias. If investors believe they have more or better information than what is reflected in the market, they have excessive confidence in their ability to generate better outcomes.

Question 8: Determine whether the investment process applied by Manager 1 is most likely fundamental or quantitative.

(Fundamental, Quantitative)

Justify your response.

General Feedback

There are two broad categories of active investment strategies: fundamental and quantitative. The fundamental approach typically begins with the analysis of a company's financial statements to gain an understanding of the company's financial position and cash flows, and provide insight into the company's business model, management team, etc. Using this information, the analyst will estimate the stock's intrinsic value and/or its relative value compared with its peer group, or its own share history. This will then guide the portfolio manager as to whether the stock should be bought or sold.

Quantitative strategies involve analyst judgement at the design stage. Once the model is designed, the reliance on human judgement is replaced with a systematic process. The systematic process searches for security and market characteristics and patterns (factors) that have predictive power in order to identify securities or trades that will earn superior investment returns, in the sense of expected added value relative to risk or expected return relative to a benchmark.

In the case of Manager 1, the investment process is described as:

"Portfolios are diversified across sectors and regions, within a risk-controlled framework. Using a proprietary model, we select from a broad universe of companies those with growth potential and attractive valuations. We further analyze growth characteristics, valuations, and cash flows to uncover companies trading at discounts from our determination of their intrinsic value. By applying this investment approach, we can best meet the needs of our clients."

We can break down the description and evaluate each part:

Portfolios are diversified across sectors and regions, within a risk-controlled framework. The description of a diversified portfolio does not give information on which process is used. Both fundamental and quantitative approaches may use statistical techniques to build optimized portfolios.

Using a proprietary model, we select from a broad universe of companies those with growth potential and attractive valuations. This indicates the investment manager is screening the investment universe. This is typically seen in fundamental managers, where they do not have the resources to analyze every company, rather they screen the universe to find potential investments and focus attention there. Quantitative models, which focus on factors and have unlimited ability to evaluate investments, are less likely to begin the investment process by screening.

We further analyze growth characteristics, valuations, and cash flows to uncover companies trading at discounts from our determination of their intrinsic value. The use of further analysis to determine intrinsic value, and use of that versus market price, is at the core of fundamental analysis. Quantitative analysis is focused on factor exposures, not intrinsic value.

By applying this investment approach, we can meet the needs of our clients. This does not provide any additional information about either investment management approach.

Question 14: Which type of algorithm is most appropriate for liquidating the Smart Suppliers position?

Arrival price algorithm

Incorrect because arrival price algorithms are used when the security is relatively liquid or the order is not outsized (e.g., the order is less than 15% of expected volume) such that a participatory strategy is not expected to result in significant market impact from order execution. This is not the case for Smart Suppliers, as the order is outsized relative to the market and the stock is illiquid (there is a high bid-ask spread).

Dark aggregator algorithm

Correct because these algorithms are used when order size is large relative to the market (i.e., a large percentage of expected volume) and when trading in the open market using arrival price or VWAP strategies would lead to significant market impact. These algorithms are appropriate for trading securities that are relatively illiquid or have relatively wide bid–ask spreads. Since trading in dark pools offers less certainty of execution (offsetting orders may never arrive), these algorithms are appropriate for trades in which the trader or portfolio manager does not need to execute the order in its entirety.

Volume-weighted average price scheduled algorithm

Incorrect because VWAP (scheduled) algorithms slice the order into smaller amounts to send to the market following a time slicing schedule based on historical intraday volume profiles. These algorithms typically trade a higher percentage of the order at the open and close and a smaller percentage of the order during midday. Because of this, the VWAP curve is said to resemble a U-shaped curve. A disadvantage of a time slicing strategy is that it will force the trades even in times of insufficient liquidity and will not take advantage of increased liquidity conditions when available. Scheduled algorithms are often appropriate when the order size is relatively small (e.g., no more than 5%–10% of expected volume), the security is relatively liquid, or the orders are part of a risk-balanced basket and trading all orders at a similar pace will maintain the risk balance.

Question 22: Explain one reason supporting Grey's conclusion that the client's duration-matched portfolio would immunize the liability under his forecast in Exhibit 1.

General Feedback

The risk to immunization is that as the yield curve shifts and twists, the cash flow yield on the bond portfolio does not match the change in the yield on the zero-coupon bond that would provide for perfect immunization. If the change in the cash flow yield is the same as that on the zero-coupon bond being replicated, immunization can be achieved even with a non-parallel shift to the yield curve.

Therefore, to explain why the portfolio would immunize the liability, we need to determine the change in the cash flow yield of the liability and verify that it matches the change in the asset portfolio's cash flow yield. The key assumption to achieve immunization is the statement that any ensuing change in the cash flow yield on the bond portfolio is equal to the change in the yield to maturity on the zero-coupon bond. Because the 5-year liability is discounted at the maturity-matched government bond yield, we can use the change in the 5-year government bond YTM derived from Exhibit 1 to determine the change in the liability cash flow yield.  

To determine the change in the yield of the 5-year government bond, we can take the forecasted 5-year corporate bond yield change and back out the forecasted change from the 5-year corporate bond spread. The difference is the change in the 5-year government bond yield.  

5-year corporate bond yields fell by 45 bps 

5-year corporate bond spreads fell by 8 bps  

= 5-year government bond yields fell by 37 bps 

As stated in the vignette, the cash flow yield of the portfolio fell by 37 bps, which is equal to the change in yield on the 5-year government bond. Thus, the portfolio would immunize the liability under Grey's forecast.

Question 23: Determine the most likely effect of:

i.  Change 1 on model risk.

    (Decrease, Increase)

ii. Change 2 on structural risk.

    (Decrease, Increase)

Justify each response.

Note: Each determination should be stated and paired with its justification, in separate paragraphs.

General Feedback

Change 1 would reduce model risk, which is encountered in financial modeling whenever assumptions are made about future events and approximations are used to measure key parameters. Modeling bonds that have call features will require additional assumptions that are not required for bonds that don't have any calls and have a single, defined maturity date. By removing high-yield callable bonds and replacing them with investment grade bonds with no call provisions, we would be reducing one source of model risk in the portfolio.  

Change 2 would increase structural risk to the strategy. Structural risk is the potential for non-parallel shifts and twists in the yield curve to lead to changes in the cash flow yield that do not track the change in the yield of the zero-coupon bond. Structural risk is reduced by minimizing the dispersion of the bond positions, going from a barbell design to more of a bullet portfolio that concentrates the component bonds' durations around the investment horizon. The duration-matching portfolio holds 50% in 5-year bonds and 40% in 8-year bonds, which is reasonably concentrated around the maturity of the liability. Reducing the market value of the 5- and 8-year bonds and increasing the market value of the 2- and 10-year bonds will increase the dispersion of the portfolio.  If the dispersion were to be increased by building this more laddered portfolio, the portfolio would not see a decrease in structural risk; rather, a laddered portfolio would increase the dispersion of holdings and increase the structural risk. 

Question 24: Discuss the effect that changing to a defeasance strategy would have on the portfolio's:

i.  price risk.

ii. reinvestment risk.

Note: Each discussion should be in a separate paragraph.

General Feedback

The client is presently immunizing with a portfolio that uses corporate bonds. Implementation of a defeasance strategy would replace the corporate bonds with a single zero-coupon government bond with a face value equal to the liability and that matures on or near the liability's due date. The client has asked how the change would affect two sources of risk, price risk and reinvestment risk.  

1. Price risk would be reduced: Price risk is the risk that the price at which a bond will be sold is unknown prior to the sale. A duration-matched portfolio will require the ongoing sale and purchase of bonds to rebalance the duration over the life of the portfolio. Further, the portfolio will have to liquidate holdings close to the liability payment date to fund the liability. A portfolio that consists of a zero-coupon bond which matures at or just prior to the maturity of the liability will have no price risk.  

2. Reinvestment risk would be reduced: The current portfolio has coupon-paying bonds that will require regular reinvestment and re-balancing. As interest rates change, the reinvestment rates will likely change and will thus impact the payoff of the immunization. A zero-coupon government bond has no reinvestment risk as there are no cash flows other than the receipt of principal at maturity, which will be used to pay off the liability.

Question 36: Formulate a strategy that would allow Grug to achieve his objectives related to the European ETF and the pharma ETF.

General Feedback

A covered call occurs when a party that already owns shares sells a call option, giving another party the right to buy their shares at the exercise price. Grug can use the existing European ETF upon which to sell a call with a strike price equal to his target price. This will allow him to generate income while he waits to reduce his holding of the European ETF at his target price.

Since Grug tells Fernandez that he wants to achieve both exposures without allocating any additional capital, there isn't any intention to make any extra funds available to buy the pharma ETF outright. Given that he wants long exposure to the pharma ETF today, he can buy a call which will enable him to benefit if the price rises, as well as lock in a price to buy the pharma ETF if the European ETF is called away and thus provides him with available cash. 

By taking this approach, he is not taking on an obligation that he can't meet (i.e., if he sold a put on the pharma ETF then he would have the obligation to buy it but may not have sufficient funds to do so if his long position in the European ETF declined in value and thus was not called away) and this can be repeated until the stock meets the target price or he changes his expectations.

Question 38: Formulate , given Fernandez's skew observation, the most appropriate options trade on the technology firm's stock.

Explain the change in the volatility curve required to make the trade profitable.

General Feedback

A volatility skew exists where the implied volatility increases for out-of-the-money puts and decreases for out-of-the-money calls as the strike price moves away from the current price. However, since Fernandez has seen that the disparity between the implied volatility of the 3-month options is far higher than historical levels, she should consider a long risk reversal trade, which is then delta hedged, with the expectation that the implied volatility for the out-of-the-money puts would fall and/or that the implied volatility of the out-of-the-money calls would rise – in other words, that the implied volatilities of OTM puts and calls would converge.

Specifically, a long risk reversal trade would be implemented with a combination of long OTM calls and short OTM puts on the same underlying with the same expiration. The options position is then delta-hedged by selling the underlying asset. Therefore, for the trade to succeed, the call options would need to rise more or decrease less in implied volatility than the put options – in other words, the call would increase in value and the put would decline in value in relative terms.
