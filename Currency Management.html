Question 1

Construct a CNY/USD carry trade. Explain two risks involved in this carry trade.

General Feedback

The carry trade is a strategy that involves borrowing in low-yield currencies and investing in high-yield currencies.

Borrow in USD at 2.211%

Invest in China at 5.667%

Profit = 5.667% – 2.211% = 3.456%

The risks involved in a carry trade include:

  • High-yielding currencies are typically from high-risk countries.
  • In times of global financial crisis, there is a rapid movement from high-risk currencies to low-risk currencies, resulting in the unwinding of carry trades.
  • Large-scale losses can be incurred quickly due to the large amount of leverage involved in a carry trade.
Question 2

Calculate the maximum gain and breakeven price per contract of a $5.00/$6.00 bull spread using BRL/USD options.

General Feedback

A bull call spread involves buying an ITM call and writing a deeper OTM call to gain income from premium. Both options have the same maturity.

Premium paid to buy BRL/USD $5.00 strike call = –$1.95

Premium received to write BRL/USD $6.00 strike call = $1.50

Net premium paid per bull call spread contract = $1.95 – $1.50 = $0.45

Maximum gain = Difference between call strike prices – Net premium paid

                           = ($6.00 – $5.00) – $0.45 = $0.55

Breakeven price = Lower strike price + Net premium paid = $5.00 + $0.45 = $5.45

Question 4

Identify which of Peters’ recommendations is most likely correct. (Recommendation 1, Recommendation 2) Justify your response.

General Feedback

Recommendation 1 is correct. During stable economic conditions, traders are often net short volatility, as most options will expire out-of-the-money (OTM) and the option writer can keep the premium as payment for accepting volatility risk. Conversely, to hedge against uncertainty during unstable economic conditions, traders often go net long volatility using options.

Recommendation 2 is incorrect. An increase in Germany’s inflation rate will cause its currency to depreciate. As this will result in investments denominated in the German currency decreasing in value, exposure to Germany should be reduced rather than increased.

Question 5

Construct a negative carry trade using the MXN/USD currency pair.

General Feedback

A negative carry trade is implemented by borrowing at high rates and investing at low rates. Traders would initiate a negative carry trade if they believed that the low-interest currency in which they are investing will appreciate significantly relative to the high-interest currency they are borrowing.

Based on Exhibit 1, the investor can borrow in MXN at 4.50% and invest in USD at 2.21%.

Net borrowing cost = Borrowing in MXN – Return in USD = 4.50% – 2.21% = 2.29%

However, the one-year MXN/USD forward rate allows the investor to lock in a profit on the exchange rate movement that exceeds the net borrowing cost of the negative carry trade.

One-year exchange rate return = [(Forward rate – Spot rate) / Spot rate] * 100%

MXN/USD spot rate = 18.90

MXN/USD forward rate = 19.72

One-year exchange rate return = 4.34%

Even though the net borrowing cost is 2.29%, the investor can make an overall profit due to the 4.34% exchange rate return in this negative carry trade.

Any two of the following four points is acceptable:

RDC=∑i=1nωi(1+RFC,i)(1+RFX,i)−1

RDC=∑i=1nωi[RFC,i+RFX,i+(RFC,i×RFX,i)]

(0.50 × 10.0%) + (0.25 × 5.0%) + [0.25 × (–3.0%)] = 5.5%.

(0.50 × 0.0%) + (0.25 × 2.0%) + (0.25 × 4.0%) = 1.5%.

[0.50 × (10.0% × 0.0%)] + [0.25 × (5.0% × 2.0%)] + [0.25 × (–3.0% × 4.0%)] = –0.005%.

General Feedback

In currency markets, volatility is not constant, nor are its movements completely random. Instead, volatility is determined by a wide variety of underlying factors, both fundamental and technical, for which a trader can express an opinion. A volatility-based strategy for Konev would typically be net short, as opposed to net long, volatility to earn the related risk premium for absorbing volatility risk. Question 53

Recognizing that market conditions have been stable since the “model” portfolio’s inception, CCM management is sensitive to the consequences of extreme market events for emerging market risk and return.

Evaluate the application of emerging market and developed market investment return probability distributions for CCM’s potential new product.

General Feedback

Emerging market currency trades are subject to relatively frequent extreme events and market stresses. Thus, return probability distributions for emerging market investments exhibit fatter tails than the normal distributions that are customarily used to evaluate developed market investment performance. Additionally, emerging market return probability distributions also have a pronounced negative skew when compared with developed market (normal) distributions.

Given these differences, risk management and control tools (such as VAR) that depend on normal distributions can be misleading under extreme market conditions and greatly understate the risks to which the portfolio is exposed. Likewise, many investment performance measures used to evaluate performance are also based on the normal distribution. As a result, historical performance evaluated by such measures as the Sharpe ratio can look very attractive when market conditions are stable, but this apparent outperformance can disappear into deep losses faster than most investors can react.

Short-term stability in emerging markets can give investors a false sense of overconfidence and thereby encourage over-positioning based on the illusion of normally distributed returns. Thus, CCM should not assume a normal distribution for its “model” emerging market portfolio. CCM should assume a fatter-tailed, negatively skewed return probability distribution better reflecting the risk exposure to extreme events.
