ASSET ALLOCATION

1 Capital Market Expectations, Part 1: Framework & Macro Considerations

Appraisal data bias: Appraised values understate volatility and correlations because they smooth over market fluctuations.

Nonstationarity issue: Structural changes in economies lead to nonstationarity, where different data periods reflect differing statistical properties.

Sample mean imprecision: Using higher-frequency data improves estimates of variances and covariances, but not the precision of the sample mean.

Asynchronous data risk: High-frequency data may be asynchronous across variables, distorting correlations and creating false lead-lag relationships.

Ex post risk bias: Risk measured after the fact can be a biased indicator of the risk expected beforehand.

Anchoring bias: Tendency to give disproportionate weight to the first information it receives on a topic.

Prudence bias: Forecasters often err on the side of caution, avoiding extreme predictions to protect reputations.

Status quo bias: Forecasts tend to mirror recent trends, avoiding change due to fear of being wrong through action rather than inaction.

Confirmation bias: Analysts often favor evidence that supports their existing beliefs and discount contradictory information.

Availability bias: Vivid or recent events disproportionately influence judgments due to ease of recall.

Data-mining bias: Arises from repeatedly searching a data set until a statistically significant pattern emerges.

Econometric model types: Structural models are theory-driven, while reduced-form models rely more on data with weaker theoretical ties.

LEI simplicity: Leading indicator–based forecasting is simple and doesn’t require exogenous variable assumptions.

Diffusion index: A diffusion index aggregates leading indicators by counting how many signal growth versus decline.

Econometric discipline: Econometric models enforce internal consistency and challenge forecasters to align views with modeled outcomes.

Econometric model error: Forecasts may be flawed if variables are mismeasured and are hampered by relationships that shift over time.

Checklist flexibility: The main strength of the checklist approach is its adaptability to changing economic structures, however, imposes no consistency and is subjective.

Inflation surprises: Unexpected changes in inflation can sharply move long-term bond yields as inflation expectations shift.

Real estate inflation link: Rental income from real estate tends to track expected inflation, with lease structure and property quality affecting sensitivity.

Automatic stabilizers: Progressive taxes and transfer payments moderate economic cycles by adjusting with growth and contraction.

Interest rate parity: With perfect capital mobility and a fixed exchange rate, interest rates must equalize across pegged currencies.

Expectational rate link: Yields tend to be higher in currencies expected to depreciate and lower in those expected to appreciate.

Weaker currency yields: Bond yields in less credible or weaker currencies are typically higher due to perceived devaluation risk.

Shared yield curve conditions: A default-free yield curve is shared between countries only if capital mobility is perfect and exchange rates are credibly fixed.

Initial recovery: Fears of a recession dissipate, short-term rates remain low and business confidence picks up.

Early expansion: Short-term rates begin to rise as borrowing increases and central banks withdraw stimulus.

Late expansion: Output gap has closed, high inflation and restrictive monetary policy.

Slowdown: Peaking short-term rates,

Contraction: Credit becomes tight and production falls before easing of monetary policy,

2 Capital Market Expectations, Part 2: Forecasting Asset Class Returns

Forecasting model categories: Forecasting capital market returns typically uses statistical methods, discounted cash flow models or risk premium models.

Statistical structure: Statistical methods impose minimal structure on data, relying on historical patterns with limited economic interpretation.

Limited economic insight: Forecasts based on statistical models reflect data properties without explaining underlying economic drivers.

Statistical method types: The three statistical methods are sample statistics, shrinkage estimation, and time-series forecasting.

Shrinkage estimation: Combines historical data with prior beliefs to reduce forecast errors. When a target VCV matrix is combined with a sample VCV matrix efficiency will increase i.e. lower MSE.

Weighted estimate method: Shrinkage estimation uses a weighted average of historical data and external inputs to improve accuracy.

Time-series dynamics: Time-series methods forecast using past values of variables, incorporating dynamics but lacking structural explanation.

YTM as proxy: Yield to maturity approximates a bond’s expected return and is commonly used despite being an oversimplification.

Fixed-income forecast approaches: Fixed-income return forecasts are based on discounted cash flow, risk premium models, or equilibrium models.

Risk premium use: Building block model, required returns has four components: the one-period default-free rate, the term premium, the credit premium and the liquidity premium.

Term premium properties: Term premiums are generally positive, grow with maturity, scale with duration, and vary over time.

Term premium inference: Term premiums cannot be directly derived from the yield curve despite its usefulness.

Term premium influences: Term premiums are shaped by inflation uncertainty and ability to hedge recession risk.

Inflation effect: Higher inflation increases both expected inflation and inflation risk, raising nominal yields and term premiums.

Equilibrium model inclusion: Including fixed income in an equilibrium model enforces consistency and supports frameworks like Black–Litterman.

Duration offset: If the investment horizon matches Macaulay duration, capital gains and reinvestment effects offset, aligning return with YTM. Reinvestment risk dominates longer horizons.

Aggregate demand link: When growth and inflation are demand-driven, bond returns are negatively correlated with growth, implying a lower term premium.

Aggregate supply link: When driven by supply, bond returns correlate positively with growth, requiring a higher term premium.

Credit premium role: The credit premium compensates for unexpected losses beyond the expected level of defaults.

Downgrade risk: Downgrade bias—downgrades being more likely and impactful than upgrades—is the main driver of credit spreads in top-rated bonds.

Bond liquidity drivers: Bond liquidity improves with pricing near par, recency, issue size, issuer reputation, structural simplicity, and credit quality.

Emerging market deficit signal: A fiscal deficit consistently above 4% or a debt-to-GDP ratio over 70–80% signals vulnerability in emerging markets.

External imbalance indicators: Current account deficits above 4% of GDP, foreign debt over 50% of GDP or 200% of current account receipts, and low reserves indicate risk.

GDP-growth link to equities: The link between equity market appreciation and GDP growth is more stable than other return components.

Gordon model stability: The Gordon growth model ties long-term equity returns to relatively stable economic growth.

REIT short-term behavior: REITs show high short-term correlation with equities due to their traded nature.

REIT long-term correlation: Over multi-year horizons, REITs are more correlated with direct real estate than with equities.

PPP principle: Purchasing power parity assumes goods prices change equally across currencies, implying long-run exchange rate alignment, poor predictor of exchange rates in the short to intermediate term.

Trade balance adjustment: Without capital flows, prices, quantities, and exchange rates must adjust to maintain trade balance.

Inflation and PPP: Relative purchasing power has an inverse effect on exchange rates and is strongest when inflation differences are large, persistent, and driven by monetary policy.

Exchange rate adjustment: Exchange rate changes must influence saving, spending, and investment decisions to affect the current account balance.

Capital and current account balance: Kept in balance by four primary mechanisms: changes in income (GDP), interest rates and asset prices, and exchange rates.

Capital account dominance: Short- and intermediate-term adjustments largely occur through the capital account due to slow changes in the current account.

Current account deficit: Net importer and investment exceeds savings, business profits and government surplus. Can be sustainable if driven by profitable investment spending.

Capital account surplus: A current account deficit can be finance by borrowing from other countries, funding the net imports in one’s own currency or a reduction in FDI.

Structural current account imbalances: Persist beyond trade adjustments arise from fiscal policy or demographics. Deficits will raise the required return to induce savings and capital, allocate assets to countries with current account surplus.

Foreign direct investment: Seen as most supportive of a currency due to long-term commitment and productivity impact.

Long-run capital flow: Global capital allocation will flow to countries with the best trend growth rates as applying downward pressure on the currency and increasing risk premiums.

Business cycle flows: Capital tends to flow to currencies of countries in strong phases of the business cycle.

Hot money outflows: Central banks will (1) uphold the exchange rate by selling their foreign currency reserves & (2) buy government bonds to restore domestic currency supply preventing interest rates from rising.

UIP assumption: Uncovered interest rate parity assumes exchange rate changes match interest rate differentials.

VCV complexity: Forecasting a full variance–covariance matrix is more complex when variances are not constant or multiple assets are involved.

Factor model structure: Factor models structure the VCV matrix by tying covariances to a few common factors, enabling use with many assets and few observations.

Factor model efficiency: A well-chosen factor model reduces estimation error and dimensionality compared to the sample VCV matrix.

Riskless portfolio avoidance: If none of the factors used in a factor-based VCV model are redundant and their returns are not completely determined by the common factors, there will not be any portfolios that erroneously appear to be riskless.

Factor model bias: Factor-based VCV matrices are biased and inconsistent, unlike the sample VCV which is unbiased and consistent.

Sample size: A sample VCV matrix will be unbiased and consistent. There should be 10 times as many observations as asset classes.

Shrinkage benefits: Shrinkage estimation combines sample and factor-based matrices to balance accuracy and efficiency.

Smoothing effects: Return smoothing understates risk and overstates diversification by distorting volatility and correlations.

ARCH models: ARCH models capture time-varying volatility patterns observed in financial returns.

Growth impact split: Higher trend growth benefits equities via earnings growth but hurts bonds by raising real interest rates.

Cycle trough signals: Equity buying is favored near business cycle troughs when term and credit premiums are high.

Market integration shift: Per Singer-Terhaar, rising market integration lowers required returns, supporting reallocations toward markets becoming more integrated.

3 Overview of Asset Allocation

Strategic allocation: Approval typically sits with the investment committee. Individual manager selection is made to achieve strategic goals, not a strategic goal itself.

Good governance best practices: Transparent decision rights, long-terms for committee members, periodic governance audits. 

Economic balance sheet: Includes assets and liabilities not included on the balance sheet such as IP, mineral resources, future tuition expenses, human capitlal. For pension funds it excludes real estate not owned by the company. For endowment funds it should include future capital projects and pledged contributions. 

Three approaches to asset allocation: (1) asset-only, (2) liability-relative, and (3) goals-based.

Goals-based allocation: Goals-based investing involves assigning each goal to its own sub-portfolio, each with a tailored asset allocation reflecting its cash flow needs, horizon, and risk tolerance.

Asset-only risk focus: Mean–variance objective approach that aims to maximize expected return per unit of volatility, the Sharpe ratio is a key metric.

Risk representation methods: Downside risk can be captured using semi-variance, peak to trough, max drawdown, or tail-focused metrics like value at risk.

Monte Carlo simulation: Mean–variance results are often enhanced with Monte Carlo simulations to better capture downside and tail risks under stress conditions.

Asset class characteristics: Homogenous, mutually exclusive, offer liquidity to absorb a large portion of the portfolio & diversifying i.e. low correlations with other classes.

Pair-wise correlations pitfall: An asset class may be highly correlated with some linear combination of the other asset classes even when pairwise correlations are not high.

Asset classes vs. strategies: Asset classes provide non-skill-based ex ante return premium, unlike strategies which depend on investment skill.

Overlap of risk factors: An asset class approach can give rise to an overlap in risk factors, such as inflation, liquidity, and volatility.

Factor-based asset classes: Asset classes can be described with respect to their sensitivities to factors, some which aren’t directly investable, involving long and short positions.

Sharpe ratio efficiency: An efficient portfolio has the highest Sharpe ratio among portfolios with equal volatility.

Dynamic allocation: Deviations from strategic allocation driven by long-term views or valuation signals are known as dynamic asset allocation.

Tactical allocation: Short-term deviations from the dynamic allocation to capture opportunities. Must weigh potential outperformance against costs such as taxes and trading expenses.

Active vs passive management: Active managers adjust portfolios based on capital market expectations, while passive managers do not.

Price drift effect: Market-driven price changes cause portfolios to drift from target allocations, necessitating rebalancing.

Rebalancing thresholds: Percent-range rebalancing defines allowable ranges for asset weights, triggering trades when limits are breached.

Risk concentration: Without rebalancing, higher-return assets grow faster and increase overall portfolio risk.

International cost impact: Higher transaction costs and correlations for international equities may justify wider rebalancing ranges, cost-benefit approach.

Risk-aversion and ranges: More risk-averse investors prefer tighter rebalancing ranges.

Correlation and ranges: Assets with lower correlations to the portfolio require tighter rebalancing control.

Trend-following views: Belief in trend following supports wider ranges, while mean reversion supports tighter rebalancing.

Synthetic rebalancing: Derivatives enable synthetic rebalancing with lower transaction costs than trading physical assets & may avoid incurring capital gains tax.

4 Principles of Asset Allocation

Markowitz diversification: Two assets not perfectly correlated can be combined to form a portfolio with risk less than the weighted average.

MVO inputs: Returns, risks (standard deviations) and pair-wise correlations.

Risk-neutral investor: A risk aversion coefficient of λ = 0 represents indifference to volatility.

Global minimum variance: The portfolio with the lowest risk on the efficient frontier is the global minimum variance portfolio.

Corner portfolios: Corner portfolios occur when an asset enters or leaves the efficient mix along the efficient frontier.

Monte Carlo simulation: Can incorporate the interaction between portfolio rebalancing and taxes.

MVO criticisms: Mean–variance optimization is sensitive to small input changes and tends to produce concentrated asset allocations.

Sensitivity to returns: Efficient portfolio composition is more sensitive to expected return estimates than to volatility or correlation estimates.

Single-period limitation: MVO does not account for trading, rebalancing costs, or taxes.

Improving MVO: Limitations of MVO can be addressed by improving input quality, constraining optimization, and treating the efficient frontier statistically.

Illiquid asset classes: Common illiquid asset classes cannot be readily diversified to eliminate idiosyncratic risk.

Reverse optimization inputs: A set of allocation weights, often market capitalisation, with inputs for covariances and risk aversion.

Black-Litterman model: Allows investors to couple their forecasts with returns from reverse optimization. Improves alignment between expected returns and systematic risk in a mean-variance framework.

Black–Litterman return inputs: accepts absolute return forecasts or relative return differentials.
Resampled mean–variance optimization: combines Monte Carlo simulation with mean–variance optimization leading to more diversified asset allocation that recognizes forward-looking inputs are subject to error.

Resampling criticisms: over-diversification in risky portfolios, inherited estimation errors, lack of theoretical foundation.

Variance as an incomplete measure of risk: More sophisticated optimizers exist that will non-normal returns, skewness, kurtosis, conditional VaR
Factor-based allocation: typical factors include market, size, valuation, momentum, liquidity, duration, credit, volatility i.e. return drivers unexplained by CAPM.

Zero-dollar investment: factors are typically self-financing positions, long the better-performing attribute, short the underperforming. This removes market exposure and correlations between.
Liability types: liabilities may be fixed or contingent; quasi-liabilities are essential future payments not legally binding.
Discounting liabilities: if liability cash flows can be perfectly hedged, the discount rate references asset discount rates.
Surplus optimization: applies MVO to surplus volatility as the measure of risk.
Surplus return definition: (Δ asset value − Δ liability value) / initial asset value; used in surplus optimization.

Hedging/return-seeking approach: separates assets into hedging and return-seeking portfolios.

Overfunded status requirement: If the funding ratio is less than one variants involving partial hedges must be used. Funded status can change with discount rates.

Single-period limitation: Surplus optimization and two-portfolio approaches struggle to assess long-term funding success due to their single-period design.

Independent asset allocation decisions: Surplus optimization and hedging are appropriate when liability decisions are made subsequent to asset allocations.

Integrated asset–liability approach: Assets and liabilities can be jointly optimized using a factor model that links underlying drivers.

Integrated approach advantages: Does not require the linear correlations assumption and can handle real-world constraints.

Goals-based advantage: Breaking portfolios into sub-portfolios improves alignment with investor goals, though it may require several refinements.

Age-based heuristic: The “120 minus your age” rule allocates a percentage to equities based on age, aligning with common glide paths.

Endowment model: “Yale” model emphasizes large allocations to non-traditional, illiquid investments with external active management and long-term horizons to earn an illiquidity premium.

Risk parity concept: Risk parity aims for equal risk contribution from each asset class or risk factor in the portfolio, however, ignores expected returns – adjusted by means of leverage.

Norway model: The Norway model emphasizes passive investing in public markets with ESG considerations, typically in a 60/40 stock/bond split.

1/N heuristic: Equal-weighting across assets ignores asset differences but often outperforms optimized methods when inputs are uncertain.

Rebalancing loss: Deviation from the strategic allocation reduces expected utility, supporting rebalancing as a corrective action.

Calendar rebalancing: Calendar-based rebalancing lowers monitoring costs compared to market-triggered rebalancing rules.

Capital gains and rebalancing: For taxable investors, higher capital gains taxes imply wider rebalancing corridors to limit taxable events.

Correlation effect on rebalancing: High correlation between asset classes reduces the likelihood of drifting from target weights, allowing wider rebalancing corridors.

5 Asset Allocation with Real-World Constraints

Governance capacity and asset complexity: Larger asset owners often have the governance capacity—sophistication and staff resources—to manage more complex asset classes.

Reallocation to scalable strategies: Large plans may shift resources from scale-sensitive asset classes like small-cap stocks to more scalable areas like private equity to maximize benefits from size.

Disadvantages of scale for managers: As AUM grows, investment managers face greater price impact, deviate from core strategies, and encounter slower decisions due to hierarchy.

Small asset owner limitations: Smaller asset owners face constrained opportunity sets primarily due to limited governance infrastructure as well as regulatory barriers.

Exploiting illiquidity premiums: Long-term investors like sovereign wealth funds can capture illiquidity premiums in private markets, unlike some pension plans constrained by liquidity needs.

Excess liquidity impact: Too much liquidity in markets can inflate asset prices and reduce expected future returns. *

Liability horizon shift with age: As the employee base ages, liabilities shift toward intermediate or short-term, requiring matching changes in asset allocation.

Extended horizon and equity allocation: A longer investment horizon and new obligations may justify a shift toward equity-oriented investments.

Mean reversion in risky assets: Risky asset returns may exhibit mean-reverting tendencies over intermediate to long horizons, supporting long-term equity investment.

Insurance capital considerations: Insurers focus on capital adequacy, yield, liquidity, and credit quality to manage risk and financial strength ratings.

Pension fund constraints: Pension asset allocations are shaped by funding, accounting, reporting, and tax constraints, which vary by jurisdiction.

Endowment risk tolerance: Endowments and foundations can typically take on higher-risk asset allocations due to perpetual horizons and flexible payout rules.

Duration extension reduces risk: Lengthening bond duration in pension portfolios can reduce balance sheet and surplus risk relative to liabilities.

Tax adjustments for allocations: After-tax and pre-tax return and standard deviations can be materially different, adjustments should be made before allocations are determined. Asset class correlations remain unchanged.

Tax effects on return distribution: Taxes reduce expected return and compress the range of outcomes by lowering highs and raising lows.

Rebalancing and tax exposure: Frequent rebalancing for taxable investors can trigger taxes. After-tax volatility is lower than pre-tax, allowing wider rebalancing bands.

Equity tax advantages: Interest income is often taxed higher than capital gains and dividends.

Asset location rule: Tax-favored assets like equities should generally be placed in taxable accounts, with high-turnover assets in tax-deferred ones—except when liquidity is needed.

Pension de-risking tension: US accounting rules discourage de-risking with bonds due to reduced assumed return, despite reduced contribution risk.

Revisiting asset allocation: Asset allocation should be reviewed regularly and in response to changes in goals, constraints, or beliefs.

Tactical allocation constraints: Success is measured by the Sharpe ratio, must stay within policy limits. Benefits to weighed against cost and tax implications.

Illusion of control bias: Overconfidence can cause investors to trade more, concentrate positions, or use tactical shifts excessively.

Loss-aversion bias: Can be mitigated in goals-based investing by framing risk as shortfall probability and matching low-risk assets to key goals.

Representativeness/recency bias: The tendency to overweight the importance of the most recent observations and information, return chasing.

Endowment bias: Investors ascribe more value to assets they own.

Availability bias: Events which come to mind more easily as considered more likely. Familiar bias leads to a home bias.

Mental accounting bias: Investors may treat different pools of money separately leading to suboptimal asset allocations on an aggregate level. Goals-based can remedy provided each allocation is on the efficient frontier.

Framing bias in decisions: The way questions are asked can influence asset allocation choices by altering perceived risk-return trade-offs.

Authority-driven bias: In group settings, early or authoritative voices can bias others’ asset allocation decisions.

PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION

1 Overview of Equity Portfolio Management

Stock lending income: Stock lenders can earn more by reinvesting cash collateral, though it carries multiple risks and costs.

Dividend compensation: Stock borrowers must compensate lenders for dividends on loaned stocks.

Predatory trading costs: Index funds face hidden costs when traders exploit index changes, affecting prices.

Shareholder engagement burdens: Engagement can be costly and create pressure for short-term results at the expense of long-term decisions.

Insider trading risk: Selective disclosure during shareholder engagement risks violating insider trading rules.

Conflict of interest: Portfolio managers may face conflicts when engaging with companies that are also investors in their funds via employee pension funds.

Activist tools: Activist investors engage via meetings, resolutions, media campaigns, and may seek board representation.

Voting rights shift: Lending shares transfers voting rights to the borrower, possibly misaligning interests.

Recalling shares for voting: Lenders may recall shares before votes to retain influence, forfeiting lending revenue.

Free rider effect: Investors can benefit indirectly from engagement by others that improves company performance.

Asset allocation importance: Asset allocation plays a more significant role than security selection in explaining return variability.

Index manager skill: Index-based manager value lies in delivering risk-adjusted returns and clear performance explanations.

Index requirements: To be investable, an index must be rules-based, transparent, and replicable.

Non-investable index example: Geometric indexes like the Value Line are hard to replicate and not practical for investing.

Float and liquidity rules: Indexes require stocks to have sufficient float and trading volume to ensure liquidity and reduce costs.

Buffering policies: Buffer zones prevent frequent stock migrations between indexes, reducing turnover and cost.

Free-float adjustment: Free-float weighting excludes shares not available for public trading to reflect investable supply.

Packeting: Splitting stock positions during reclassification smooths transitions and reduces trading costs.

Mean–variance efficiency: A cap-weighted index portfolio may approximate mean–variance efficiency.

Liquidity proxy: Cap-weighted indexes often favor highly liquid large-cap stocks, easing large investor flows.

Free-float dominance: The dominant cap-weighting method adjusts for shares unavailable to public investors.

Price-weighted index: In a price-weighted index, each stock’s weight is based on its price, effectively treating the portfolio as holding one share of each company.

Equal weighting volatility: Equal-weighted indexes show higher volatility than cap-weighted ones due to small-cap bias.

Mispricing advantage: Equal-weighted portfolios can outperform cap-weighted ones as mispriced stocks move toward intrinsic value.

Shared index traits: Market-cap and fundamentally weighted indexes share low cost, rule-based design, transparency, and investability.

HHI measure: The Herfindahl–Hirschman Index is a valid tool for assessing stock concentration risk in portfolios.

2 Overview of Fixed-Income Portfolio Management

Mandates overview: Fixed-income mandates include liability-based and total return mandates, with portfolio managers using diverse instruments across issuers, maturities, and jurisdictions.

High-yield volatility: Lower-credit-quality bonds can behave like equities during financial stress due to increased default risk and volatility.

Real vs nominal volatility: Inflation-linked bonds exhibit lower return volatility than conventional bonds due to the lower volatility of real interest rates.

Diversification benefits: Adding fixed-coupon bonds to a portfolio with equities can lower overall volatility due to their negative correlation.

Contingent immunization: Liability-based mandates may include contingent immunization, allowing active management when assets exceed liabilities.

Active duration bets: Active managers may deviate significantly from benchmark duration to exploit market opportunities, often resulting in higher turnover and fees.

Active risk definition: Active risk refers to the annualized standard deviation of active returns, also known as tracking error or benchmark risk.

Enhanced indexing: Enhanced indexing closely tracks benchmark risk factors but allows small sector or quality mismatches to seek outperformance.

Key rate duration: Key rate durations measure a bond’s sensitivity to yield curve changes at specific maturity segments i.e. non-parallel shifts.

Macaulay duration: Macaulay duration is the weighted average time to receive a bond’s promised payments.

Empirical duration: A measure of interest rate sensitivity found by regressing bond prices to interest rates.

Effective convexity: Effective convexity measures the secondary price effect of a shift in the benchmark yield curve using a pricing model.

Coupon vs zero-coupon convexity: Coupon-paying bonds have more convexity than zero-coupon bonds of the same duration due to wider cash flow dispersion.

Zero-coupon bond properties: For zero-coupon bonds, Macaulay duration increases linearly with maturity, and convexity is roughly proportional to duration squared.

Convexity and volatility: Convexity becomes more valuable as yield volatility increases, justifying lower yield-to-maturity and higher prices in high-convexity portfolios.

Convexity and options: Portfolios with short option positions or embedded options may experience amplified convexity effects.

Spread duration: Bond price sensitivity to credit spread changes.

Duration times spread: DTS adjusts spread duration by current credit spread, accounting for spread changes occurring proportionally across the credit spectrum.

Top-down and bottom-up: Actively managed total return mandates use a top-down view for macro risks and a bottom-up approach for security selection.

Yield curve strategy: If rates are expected to rise and the curve to steepen, reducing exposure to long-duration bonds can help outperform benchmarks.

OTC bond markets: Fixed-income markets are typically over-the-counter, with higher search costs due to lack of centralized trading. Markets are less transparent than equities.

Outdated pricing risk: Relying on stale bond prices can lead to poor decisions; matrix pricing offers a more accurate valuation method.

Liquidity vs yield tradeoff: Investors seeking higher returns may accept illiquid bonds with higher yields, while liquidity-focused investors accept lower yields.

Dealer inventory risk: Bond dealers hold inventory to match order timing but face higher risk and costs with illiquid bonds, reflected in wider bid–ask spreads.

Credit complexity: Higher bid–ask spreads reflect bond complexity and difficulty in assessing issuer creditworthiness.

Mutual fund liquidity: Bond mutual funds allow investors to redeem at NAV, offering better liquidity than directly selling individual bonds which trade in large minimum lot sizes.

OTC derivatives: Interest rate swaps and other OTC derivatives offer customizable fixed-income exposure tailored to investor needs.

Total return swaps (TRS): TRS blends features of interest rate swaps and credit derivatives.

TRS cash flows: TRS receivers get both the cashflows and appreciation from the reference obligation in exchange for a MRR + spread and any losses/depreciations.

TRS flexibility: Lower upfront cost and narrow bid-offer spread than direct bond investment or mutual fund/ETF are attractive features.

Regulatory impact: Increased costs have arisen from need to collateralize.

Counterparty risk in TRS: TRS users face counterparty credit risk and rollover risk when contracts mature.

Expected vs realized return: Strategy assessment should focus on expected rather than realized returns for more forward-looking portfolio management.

Coupon income: Includes interest on reinvestment of coupon income.

Roll down return: Rolling yield combines coupon income with rolldown return, which is the expected price change assuming a stable yield curve.

Yield/spread expectation components: Uses modified duration unless there are options in which case effective duration is used. Zero impact if no expectation of change.

YTM reinvestment assumption: Return decomposition model assume bond cash flows are reinvested at yield to maturity, which varies across bonds.

Capital gains taxation: Interest income is taxed at regular rates, while capital gains may be taxed more favorably, depending on jurisdiction and investment vehicle.

Fund-level taxation: In pooled vehicles, interest income is usually taxed at the investor level when earned, regardless of reinvestment.

LDI vs ADL: Liability-driven investing manages assets against liabilities which are given, while asset-driven liabilities structure liabilities to align with given assets.

Variable-rate preference: Firms with revenues tied to the business cycle often prefer variable-rate liabilities to align income and interest expenses, in sync with monetary policy.

Portfolio shapes: Bullet portfolios focus maturities at one point; barbells split them between short and long terms; ladders spread maturities evenly across the curve.

Ladder reinvestment: Laddered portfolios balance reinvestment and price risk by reinvesting maturing bonds at longer durations. Provides liquidity as a bond will always be close to maturity.

Convexity and structure: For equal duration, barbell portfolios have the most convexity, bullets the least; ladders provide moderate convexity with lower reinvestment risk.

3 Asset Allocation to Alternative Investments

Private equity valuation and risk: Private equity assets are valued at lower of cost or recent capital raise, masking true volatility; proxies are used to estimate risk due to limited diversification and unobservable pricing.

PE considerations: Capital calls highest at an economic drawdown as investment opportunities arise, GP will avoid selling investments. Little transparency.

PE qualitative criteria: Key person risk, style drift, alignment of interests, client turnover.

Private equity fee drag: Fees based on committed rather than invested capital can amplify the J-curve effect, making early negative returns hard to recover.

Hedge fund strategy spectrum: Hedge funds range from risk reducers to return enhancers, with strategies like arbitrage, long/short equity, and global macro offering varied volatility and correlation profiles.

Short-biased hedge funds: Short-biased strategies aim to reduce a portfolio’s equity beta while generating alpha.

Inflation-linked real assets: Physical assets like timber, farmland, and energy show high correlation with inflation or its components.

Direct lending: Behave like bonds as income producing assets.

Distressed investing: Resembling equity with managers playing an active role in restructuring. Show low sensitivity to traditional bond risks due to dominant idiosyncratic company risk.

Short-term volatility focus: For short-term investors, volatility is the key risk metric, unlike long-term investors who focus on achieving return targets.

Long-term return concern: For long-horizon investors, the main risk is failing to meet long-term return objectives, not short-term volatility.

Hedge fund database bias: Back-fill bias in hedge fund databases may understate downside risk by including only successful returns.

Diversification via correlation: Lower correlation improves diversification power, while beta reflects sensitivity to market changes.

Traditional approach: Assets are classified to liquidity nature or macroeconomic regime i.e. capital growth, inflation hedging. Classes might share risk factors and not be diversified.

Risk-based approach: Public and private assets are grouped by risk factors and use an integrated risk factor framework. Challenges determining factors and how to measure, factor sensitivity subject to lookback period & establishing targets.

Alternatives vs. modeled allocation: Actual allocations in alternatives rarely match modeled allocations due to delayed capital deployment and return of capital, need for ongoing monitoring.

MVO allocation bias: Mean–variance optimization tends to over-allocate to alternatives due to higher expected returns and understated risk. Constrain allocations to alternatives or CVaR optimization.

Monte Carlo modeling: Can accommodate the non-normalness, skewness and kurtosis more pronounced in alternative asset return distributions owing to information asymmetric performance fees, leverage & sensitivity to liquidity events.

Detecting smoothing bias: Serial correlation testing can reveal artificially smoothed returns in private and hedge fund investments, which must be unsmoothed for accurate modeling.

4 An Overview of Private Wealth Management

Life-cycle framework: Private wealth management is structured around the life-cycle view of human capital, influencing investment decisions through evolving personal and financial circumstances.

Investable net worth: Investable net worth consists of the sum of liquid assets, typically after the sale of a business or similar liquidity event.

Gini coefficient: The Gini coefficient measures wealth inequality, ranging from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (complete inequality), based on deviations from equal distribution.

Longevity risk: In late retirement, individuals face longevity risk—the danger of outliving their financial resources—along with cognitive decline and potential healthcare or caregiving needs.

Human capital: Future income discounted at the nominal risk-free rate (includes inflation) + premium for income volatility.

Unplanned financial goals: Unplanned financial needs are difficult to quantify due to uncertain timing or amounts, often arising from unexpected expenses like medical or property costs.

Capital gains basis: Capital gains are taxed on the realized difference between selling price and the initial investment (basis), with some jurisdictions taxing unrealized gains and allowing deferral through special accounts.

Tax drag: Compounds over time in accrual taxation, difference between annual tax and zero tax as a percentage of zero tax earnings.

Tactical allocation: Tactical asset allocation is a short-term strategy involving frequent portfolio adjustments to exploit market trends, requiring greater skill and higher costs.

Wealth manager duties: A wealth manager’s responsibilities span a range of services tailored to individual client needs.

Proactive allocation: Asset allocation strategies can be proactive, adjusting to changing conditions to manage risk and returns.

Urgent IPS review: A significant change in portfolio value should prompt an immediate review of the Investment Policy Statement (IPS).

5 Portfolio Management for Institutional Investors

PBGC role in pension plans: The PBGC collects premiums and pays benefits in terminated plans, supporting participants when employers end plans under ERISA regulation.

Pension Protection Act: Provides the maximum discount rate for liabilities is high-grade bond yields over the preceding 25 years.

Discount rates: Required return for plan assets should exceed the discount rate on plan liabilities. Consequently, forces DB plans to target greater investment returns, increasing portfolio risk.

DB plan funding on balance sheet: U.S. accounting rules require overfunded or underfunded DB plans to appear as assets or liabilities on corporate balance sheets.

Income statement impact: Pension gains, losses, and service costs affect net income under ASC 715, driving adoption of liability-driven strategies.

Public pension accounting incentives: GASB rules allow higher discount rates for funded liabilities, encouraging public plans to favor equities and alternatives.

Canada model: Focus on alternatives led by internal investment teams (in contrast to endowment/Yale model).

Liability-driven model: Finite horizon, insurance firms, banks & DB funds.

Pension plan risk tolerance: Higher tolerance if funded, small in comparison to sponsor company, low correlation with operating results.

6 Trading Costs and Electronic Markets

Inside spread: The market bid–ask spread, or inside spread, is tighter than individual dealer spreads when best bids and asks come from different dealers.

Implementation shortfall: Implementation shortfall measures total trading cost from an investment perspective, including explicit, implicit, market impact, delay, and opportunity costs.

Liquidity aggregators: Aggregators consolidate liquidity across venues, giving traders a global view and enabling smart order routing.

Quote matchers: Quote matchers front-run liquidity suppliers by quickly trading based on observed orders.

Quote-matching risk: Quote-matching strategies risk failure if standing orders are filled or canceled before use.

Leapfrogging: Wide spreads encourage dealers to improve quotes competitively, causing a leapfrogging dynamic that frustrates buy-side traders.

Flickering quotes: Rapidly canceled orders used to signal willingness to trade without committing to it.

Machine learning limits: Machine-learning trading systems often fail during volatility spikes due to lack of precedent and increased risk.

Bluffing: Manipulates perceived value by placing misleading orders, often targeting momentum traders.

Spoofing/Layering: misleads others about market liquidity or value by placing exposed orders to trigger trades at favorable prices.

Wash trading: Wash trading fakes market activity via trades between commonly controlled accounts to falsely suggest liquidity and confidence.

Gunning the market: Market manipulators gun the market by selling rapidly to trigger stop-loss orders and buy back at lower prices.

Buy-side order systems: Electronic buy-side systems reduced costs by enabling fewer traders to handle more orders efficiently.

Bond market lag: Electronic trading revolutionized equity markets but has had limited impact on corporate and municipal bonds, which remain OTC.

Systematic risk controls: Trading halts imposed when prices move too quickly do not prevent high frequency traders competitive advantage, delaying order prices will.

PERFORMANCE MEASUREMENT

1 Portfolio Performance Evaluation

Ex post risk: Ex post techniques assess historical portfolio risk using metrics like volatility and performance ratios.

Attribution purpose: Attribution explains how performance or risk occurred by decomposing returns or risks into active manager decisions and unintended exposures.

Performance appraisal: Appraisal uses risk, return, and attribution to judge performance quality and distinguish skill from luck.

Ex ante risk: VaR is an example of ex ante risk, assessing potential future losses at a point in time.

Performance measurement: Measures return relative to a benchmark and forms the basis for understanding excess return.

Macro attribution use: Attribution evaluates tactical asset allocation and manager selection decisions by the asset owner.

Deviation attribution: Attribution quantifies effects from deviations between strategic asset allocation and implementation.

Returns-based attribution: Used when holdings data are insufficient, this method is easiest but least accurate and vulnerable to manipulation.

Equity attribution types: Common approaches include Brinson–Fachler and factor-based attribution.

Multi-period arithmetic: Arithmetic attribution aligns with excess return in single periods but needs smoothing across periods due to geometric linking.

Selection effect: Selection assesses whether excess return came from outperforming securities or avoiding underperformers.

Allocation effect: Allocation evaluates whether overweighting outperforming or underweighting underperforming categories drove return.

Arithmetic preference: Chosen for simplicity and intuitive results, arithmetic attribution suits clients and marketing use.

Positive sector overweight: Overweighting a positively returning sector generates a positive allocation effect even if it underperforms the benchmark.

Negative outperformers: Overweighting a negative-return sector that beats the benchmark still produces a positive effect.

Selection alpha: Active return from selection is the portion not explained by factor sensitivities.

Intentional focus: Minimal selection impact isn’t negative if the manager emphasizes sector or factor allocation over security selection.

Exposure decomposition: A top-down approach attributing performance by decomposing risk exposures like duration or sector bets.

Brinson-like grouping: Exposure decomposition groups assets by duration buckets, similar to Brinson’s equity models.

Simplified reporting: Exposure decomposition is favored for marketing due to easy data requirements and understandable results.

Yield decomposition structure: This approach can be top-down or bottom-up and estimates returns using duration and yield changes.

Transparency vs. complexity: Yield decomposition offers more transparency but is more operationally complex than exposure models.

Tracking risk source: Risk attribution identifies volatility or tracking risk depending on mandate type.

Funding status tracking: Attribution helps asset owners assess progress toward full funding or performance relative to liabilities.

Index vs. liabilities: A portfolio can outperform a market index but still fail to meet its liabilities.

Benchmark mismatch: Cap-weighted indexes often have shorter durations than pension liabilities, making them unsuitable benchmarks.

Credit risk in indexes: Fixed-income indexes may be overly weighted toward short-term corporate bonds, increasing undesired credit risk.

Hedge fund universe flaws: Hedge fund peer groups face issues like non-representative strategies, survivorship bias, backfill bias, and self-reported data.

Treynor vs Sharpe: Treynor requires a market benchmark to measure systematic risk, unlike Sharpe which can be used across funds.

Information ratio: IR evaluates performance relative to benchmark, scaled by tracking risk.

Sortino penalty: Sortino ratio only penalizes returns below a user-defined threshold, unlike Sharpe which penalizes all volatility.

Capital preservation: Sortino is most relevant when protecting capital is a key objective.

Skewness sensitivity: Sortino ratio effectively assesses performance for asymmetrical return distributions.

Sortino advantage: Sortino is better suited to skewed strategies like hedge funds or commodity trading funds.

Capture ratio meaning: A capture ratio above 1 indicates a convex (positively asymmetric) return profile.

Return shape interpretation: As benchmark returns rise, convex profiles see increasing portfolio returns, while concave profiles rise at a decreasing rate.

2 Investment Manager Selection

Manager fit to policy: Selection focuses on which manager best implements the asset allocation and IPS decisions.

Investment process evaluation: Due diligence assesses whether a manager’s process consistently generates expected returns from expected sources.

IPS defines search scope: The IPS and search rationale shape the manager universe and benchmark, guiding whether to complement, replace, or expand strategies.

Type II error measurement: Type II errors are hard to measure and less clearly linked to compensation.

Error cost distinction: Type I errors cause explicit costs; Type II errors cause opportunity costs, which are perceived as less painful.

Long/short implementation: Long/short strategies should be size-matched, dollar and beta neutral to minimize market risk and isolate alpha.

Most liquid funds: Closed-end funds and ETFs are most liquid due to intraday tradability, though thin markets may cause price uncertainty.

Limited partnership liquidity: Hedge funds, venture capital, and private equity funds require long-term commitments with complex redemption restrictions.

Lockup features: Lockups prevent early redemptions—hard locks disallow them completely; soft locks impose a fee.

Liquidity trade-offs: Partnership liquidity limits reduce flexibility but support long-term investing and stability during crises.

Symmetrical fee exposure: A symmetrical fee structure fully exposes managers to both upside and downside performance.

Symmetry and risk: Symmetrical fees align incentives but are unpopular with managers due to increased bankruptcy risk.

3 Overview of the Global Investment Performance Standards

Prospective client definition: A prospective client is anyone expressing interest in a firm’s strategy and eligible to invest via a segregated account.

Prospective investor definition: A prospective investor is anyone interested and eligible to invest in one of the firm’s pooled funds.

GIPS core principles: GIPS standards are built on fair representation and full disclosure of a firm’s investment performance.

GIPS objectives: GIPS aims to promote investor trust, ensure data consistency, establish global standards, foster fair competition, and support self-regulation.

Limited distribution fund: Limited distribution pooled funds, unlike broad distribution ones, are not publicly offered and include many hedge or private equity funds.

Compliance requirement: Firms must fully meet all applicable GIPS requirements before claiming compliance, with no exceptions.

Composite return: A composite return is the asset-weighted average return of all portfolios sharing the same investment mandate within the composite.

Separate division autonomy: A division with its own investment team, traders, and admin functions may independently claim GIPS compliance.

Shared investment process: If divisions share investment processes and personnel, neither can independently claim GIPS compliance.

Composite inclusion rule: All discretionary, fee-paying segregated accounts must be included in at least one composite.

Discretionary portfolio: A portfolio is discretionary if the manager can implement the intended investment strategy without restrictions.

Discretionary vs. non-discretionary: Only discretionary portfolios are included in composites, though both types are part of total firm assets.

Large cash flows: If a portfolio receives a large intra-month cash flow and returns aren’t calculated daily, a sub-period return must be calculated.

TWR calculation: TWR must be calculated monthly for most portfolios, while private market investments must be valued quarterly.

Private market valuation: Illiquid investments like real estate and private equity must be valued quarterly under GIPS.

MWR usage: MWRs may be presented instead of TWRs when the firm controls cash flows and portfolios are illiquid or fixed-life.

Transaction costs: Returns must be calculated net of transaction costs, including commissions and fees tied to investment activity.

Non-transaction fees: Some fees, such as custody fees, should not be treated as transaction costs.

Short-period returns: Returns for periods under one year must not be annualized to avoid misleading extrapolation.

Inactive markets: When transaction costs can’t be identified due to bundled fees, gross returns must be reduced by the full bundle for institutional composites.

Model portfolios: Model or hypothetical portfolios may not be included in composites or linked to actual performance.

Supplemental info: Supplemental data may be shown but cannot be blended with actual composite or pooled fund returns.

Terminated portfolios: Portfolios must remain in composites through the last full period where the firm had investment discretion.

Loss of discretion: Once discretion is lost due to termination, the portfolio must be removed from the composite after the last full period.

Composite switching policy: Firms must document when portfolios can move between composites, and historical performance must stay with the original composite.

Minimum portfolio removal: If a portfolio is removed from a composite for falling below the minimum, its past performance must remain in the composite.

Temporary removal for cash flows: A portfolio may be temporarily removed from a composite in the event of significant cash flows.

Minimum performance history: Firms must present at least 5 years of annual performance (or since inception), building to a minimum of 10 years.

Performance portability – team: Past performance may be linked if nearly all investment decision makers join the acquiring firm and maintain the same process.

Performance portability – records: Linking past performance also requires supporting records and an unbroken performance track.

Annual performance reporting: Firms must report at least five years of annual performance (or since inception), extending to ten years over time.

DERIVATIVES

1 Options Strategies

Contract size: Exchange-traded options typically trade in contracts covering 100 shares.

Implied volatility: Implied volatility equates a pricing model to the market price of an option using observable inputs.

Delta definition: Delta measures how an option’s price changes with the underlying asset; it’s positive for long calls and negative for long puts.

Vega definition: Vega measures an option’s sensitivity to changes in volatility and is always positive for long calls and puts ~ curvature.

Gamma definition: Gamma measures the rate of change of delta with the underlying price and is positive for long calls and puts.

Covered call vs protective put delta: Covered calls and protective puts have similar deltas, combining +100 from the stock with −50 from the option.

Delta sensitivity: Some options are more sensitive to the underlying asset than others; delta captures this sensitivity and varies with moneyness.

ATM call delta: At-the-money options have a delta of approximately 0.5, impacting the overall portfolio delta depending on long or short positions.

Portfolio delta: A covered call and a protective put both yield a position delta of 50 when combining long stock with offsetting ATM options.

Money spread: A money spread involves buying and selling options with the same expiration but different strike prices.

Same expiration spread: Option spreads often involve two options with the same expiration but different strikes to create payoff differences.

Straddle: an option combination in which one buys both puts and calls, with the same exercise price and same expiration date

Strangle strategy: implemented by buying OTM puts and OTM calls with the same expiration date.

Bull spread: A bull spread gains value when the underlying asset rises.

Debit spread: Debit spreads require a cash outflow and are effectively long positions.

Zero-cost collar: A zero-cost collar combines buying a put and selling a call with matching premiums.

Collar structure: A collar combines long stock with (1) a lower strike price put to protect downside and (2) write a call with a higher strike price to reduce costs.

Time decay: Time decay is more pronounced for short-term options than long-term ones, a feature exploited in calendar spreads.

Long calendar spread: involve writing a near dated option and buying a long-dated options with the same strike.

Ideal outcome: the short-term option expires near strike. If OTM the long-dated loses intrinsic value, if ITM long-dates gains less value than short-term – short term options have higher gamma.

Calendar spreads are long vega: if implied volatility of the long option rises more than short, profit increases.

Bullish calendar use: A trader with long-term bullish and short-term neutral views can use a long calendar spread to benefit from time decay.

Short calendar spread: A short calendar spread involves buying the near-term option and selling the longer-dated one.

Short calendar benefit: A short calendar spread benefits from large underlying moves or falling implied volatility.

Volatility smile: implied volatilities of OTM options exceed those of ATM options.

Volatility skew: Implied volatility often increases as puts move OTM and decreases calls move OTM.

Volatility term structure: Longer-term options generally have higher implied volatilities, though this can invert during market stress.

Risk reversal strategy: A long risk reversal involves selling an OTM put, buying an OTM call – long upside skew as it’s cheaper. Once delta-hedged not a directional bet.

Risk reversal rationale: Traders use risk reversals to profit from relative changes in implied volatility between puts and calls.

Delta approximation: Delta approximates an option’s price change for small moves in the underlying asset.

Covered call position: A covered call consists of long stock and a short call, resulting in reduced portfolio delta.

Put-call delta parity: For options on the same underlying with identical inputs, call delta minus put delta equals 1.

Bull vs bear spread: Bull spreads profit from rising markets, bear spreads from falling ones.

Put bear spread: Buying a higher-strike put and selling a lower-strike put, initial cash outflow.

Debit vs credit spread: Debit spreads require a cash outflow; credit spreads result in a cash inflow.

Call bear spread: Buying a higher-strike call and selling a lower-strike call results in a credit spread.

Protective collar: Collars use a put for downside protection and a call to offset its cost.

Collar payoff: A collar limits gains and losses by combining long stock with an OTM put and OTM call.

Calendar spread goal: Calendar spreads aim to profit from differing time decay rates between options.

Theta decay: Short-term options lose time value faster than long-term ones.

Calendar spread setup: A calendar spread buys and sells options with matching strikes but different maturities.

Bullish calendar: A trader bullish long-term and neutral short-term buys a long-dated call and sells a near-term call.

Theta advantage: A long calendar spread gains from faster decay of the short-dated option.

Short calendar construction: A short calendar involves buying a short-term option and selling a longer-dated one.

Short calendar theta: For ITM options, short-term options may decay slower than longer ones, motivating a short calendar.

Calendar spread drivers: Long spreads benefit from stable markets and rising volatility; short spreads from volatility drops or big moves.

Volatility smile: When OTM puts and calls have higher implied volatilities than ATM options, the curve forms a smile.

Volatility skew pattern: Implied volatility typically increases for OTM puts and decreases for OTM calls.

Skew persistence: Volatility skew persists due to investor demand for protective puts and disinterest in OTM calls.

Volatility contango: Longer-term options often have higher implied volatility than short-term ones.

Inverted term structure: During market stress, short-term implied volatility can exceed long-term.

Risk reversal hedge: Risk reversals combine OTM options and are delta-hedged using the underlying.

Relative volatility trade: Profit from a risk reversal comes from relative implied volatility shifts, not overall level changes.

Vega neutrality: Risk reversals can be structured to be vega-neutral, depending on the strikes used.

2 Swaps, Forwards, and Futures Strategies

Interest rate swaps: Interest rate swaps are used to manage short-term interest rate risk, adjust portfolio duration, and convert floating-rate obligations to fixed-rate ones.

Offset loans: hedge future loans by selling IRS future, positive payoff when future price decreases.

Portfolio risk adjustment with swaps: Swaps can be used alongside futures to alter a bond portfolio’s risk and return profile.

Reducing duration with swaps: Paying fixed and receiving floating in an interest rate swap creates a negative-duration position, helping reduce portfolio duration without selling assets.

Buying the basis: Traders profit from a negative basis by buying the bond and shorting the futures.

Basis arbitrage strategy: buy the basis if negative by purchasing the bond and shorting the corresponding futures contract.

Futures to reduce duration: The number of futures contracts needed is calculated to reduce portfolio modified duration to a target level.

Equity swap settlement: Equity swaps on single stocks can be settled in cash or physically, with gains depending on stock price movements at maturity.

Beta adjustment with futures: Equity index futures are used to adjust a portfolio’s beta to a desired target.

Beta hedge formula: The target beta equals the portfolio beta plus the beta exposure from futures contracts.

Portfolio value reference: S represents the market value of the equity portfolio.

Volatility as an asset class: Volatility is increasingly treated as an asset class and used to hedge against equity market downturns.

Volatility and equities: There is a pronounced negative correlation between volatility and equity index returns during market sell-offs.

Cash equitization: Cash equitization uses futures to replicate market exposure for idle cash holdings and enhance returns.

Roll return and hedging: Futures-based volatility hedges are affected by the term structure and roll returns, which vary with market conditions.

Volatility convexity: Swap payoffs increase or decrease with realized volatility, making them useful for hedging tail risk.

Effective VIX hedge: A long VIX futures position protects an equity portfolio only if implied volatility rises more than expected.

VIX futures convergence: VIX futures prices converge to the spot VIX at expiration.

VIX futures roll: The daily roll is the difference between front-month VIX futures and the VIX spot price.

Roll-down risk: Long positions in back-month VIX futures incur losses when the term structure is in contango.

VIX call options: VIX calls are purchased when a trader expects equity market volatility to rise due to a significant sell-off.

Variance swap payoff: In a variance swap, the buyer pays the difference between the fixed strike variance and realized variance over the contract period.

Variance swap convexity: The convex payoff profile of variance swaps appeals to investors seeking long volatility exposure for tail risk hedging, other hedges are linear.

Effective Federal Funds (FFE) rate: reflect the rate actually transacted between depository institutions in overnight markets, not the Fed’s target rate.

FFE rate dip effect: End-of-month institutional activity increases futures price, depressing FFE and reducing the likligood of a hike.

Smoothing EOM dips: Data providers smooth EOM dips by building a forward rate curve and adjusting implied rates for Fed Open Market Committee (FOMC) and non-FOMC months.

3 Currency Management: An Introduction

Strangle structure: A long strangle involves buying OTM puts and calls with equal distance from the current price and same expiry, offering a cheaper but less sensitive volatility bet than a straddle.

Volatility premium: Option writers often earn a steady risk premium from collecting premiums on options that expire worthless, effectively generating income for absorbing volatility risk.

PORTFOLIO-MANAGEMENT I

1 Index-Based Equity Strategies

Factor strategy risks: Factor-based strategies concentrate risk and are easily replicated, increasing the risk of crowding and underperformance when specific factors fall out of favor.

Tracking error in benchmarks: Using both factor-based and broad-market benchmarks can cause tracking error for investors referencing only broad-market indexes.

Factor strategy costs: Factor-based strategies typically have modest fees due to their rules-based nature, avoiding the higher costs of continuous active management.

Index mutual fund advantages: Benefits of index-based mutual funds include low costs and operational convenience through structured fund management.

ETF leverage: ETF shares can be bought on margin, enabling leveraged exposure for investors.

Authorized participant role: In ETFs, the authorized participant acts as a market maker and intermediary for share creation and redemption.

ETF shorting: Investors can take short positions in ETFs, adding flexibility to trading strategies.

ETF creation/redemption: The ETF creation/redemption process supports investor demand and large redemptions through in-kind transfers of underlying securities.

ETF tax efficiency: ETFs are more tax-efficient than mutual funds due to in-kind redemptions avoiding realized capital gains.

In-kind redemptions: Capital gains are minimized in ETFs through in-kind delivery of securities during redemptions.

Custom portfolios: Large investors often replicate indexes using strategies like stratified sampling and optimization for cost effectiveness.

Clearing house role: Using a clearing house for exchange-traded derivatives significantly reduces default risk.

Futures maintenance: Futures positions require daily settlement to maintain margin compliance.

Index exposure via derivatives: Derivatives allow portfolio managers to adjust full index exposure efficiently in a single transaction.

Basis risk: Basis risk arises when futures prices do not track spot index prices perfectly, potentially affecting hedging effectiveness.

Swap settlement risk: Frequent settlement in equity swaps reduces counterparty risk by limiting the potential for default losses.

Stratified sampling: Stratified sampling divides index constituents into distinct, exhaustive subgroups to closely match index characteristics using fewer securities, appropriate when the index has many constituents or working with a small asset volume.

Index optimization: Optimization minimizes tracking error or accounts for covariance under constraints like security count or market cap thresholds.

Inefficiency risk: Optimizing solely to minimize tracking error can result in portfolios that are inefficient on a mean–variance basis.

Securities lending: Securities-lending income can improve portfolio returns or offset management costs, especially in low-cost indexed portfolios.

2 Active Equity Investing: Strategies

GARP strategy: GARP blends growth and value investing by targeting firms with strong growth at reasonable valuations, often using the PEG ratio to assess value relative to growth expectations.

Factor definition: A factor is any variable correlated with asset returns and used to rank stocks or predict risk/return, though not all factors consistently generate a premium.

Factor prediction goal: Factor-based strategies aim to identify variables that help forecast future stock returns and tilt portfolios toward them.

Timing factor exposure: Some strategies attempt to time factor exposure, recognizing that factor effectiveness varies over time.

Equity style rotation: Equity style rotation reallocates among factor styles like size or momentum, depending on which is expected to outperform at a given time.

Factor basket allocation: Style rotation strategies allocate to baskets of stocks representing each factor style, adjusting allocations based on expected performance.

Timing expected returns: Style rotation relies on identifying when a factor style is likely to deliver positive excess returns relative to the benchmark.

Factor ranking: The hedged portfolio method ranks stocks by a chosen factor and divides them into quantiles for long/short strategies.

Long/short quantile portfolio: Hedged portfolios go long the top-ranked quantile and short the bottom to isolate the effect of a factor.

Linear return assumption: A key limitation of hedged portfolios is the assumption that factor-return relationships are linear, which may not hold.

Concentrated portfolios: Hedged portfolios can lead to concentration in specific stocks, especially when many managers use the same factors.

Long-only factor tilt: Investors seeking long-only exposure can build factor-tilting portfolios that maintain benchmark proximity while gaining factor exposure.

Sector-neutral momentum: Downside risk in momentum strategies can be reduced by removing sector effects, improving performance consistency.

Momentum tail risk: Momentum strategies are vulnerable to extreme losses during reversals.

Activist investing trigger: Activist campaigns begin when an investor buys a stake in a company and advocates for change to unlock value.

Underutilized assets: Identifying underused assets provides activist investors with opportunities to improve company efficiency and performance.

Mean reversion trades: Pairs trading exploits temporary divergences in historically correlated stocks by betting on return to the mean, use stop loss orders to control risk the divergence is not temporary.

Limit order book: high frequency traders can arbitrage short-term mispricing caused by temporary order imbalances.

Value trap risk: A low P/E or book value may signal a value trap if poor future prospects keep prices depressed.

Growth trap scenario: Even if a growth company meets expectations, its stock may not rise if it was overpriced initially.

Pearson Information Coefficient: Linear coeffienct of current period factor score and next period’s return, measures preditive power - an average monthly IC of 5%–6% is considered very strong.

Spearman IC: A more robust alternative less sensitive to outliers by using ranked scores.

Holdings-based accuracy: Holdings-based style analysis is generally more accurate because it evaluates actual stock positions and their style attributes. Assesses growth/value mix.

Returns-based limitation: When portfolio holdings are undisclosed, returns-based analysis is used to infer style from historical returns, accuracy heavily depends on selecting appropriate style indexes.

Returns-based flexibility: Returns-based models are more broadly applicable and easier to implement, even though holdings-based analysis gives deeper insights.

Look-ahead bias: Arises when using information unknown at the time such as estimated earnings.

Survivorship bias: Occurs when back-testing uses companies that are in business today but ignores companies that have left the investment universe.

3 Active Equity Investing: Portfolio Construction

Discretionary approach: searches for active returns from firm-specific factors, such as pricing power and the competitive landscape.

Systematic approach: designed around extracting premiums from a balanced exposure to known, rewarded factors and typically incorporates research-based rules.

Rewarded factors: Refers to risks like market or liquidity risk that investors expect to earn a long-term premium for bearing, often mistaken historically as alpha.

Strategic factor exposure: Managers create long-term exposure to rewarded risks through strategic active weight adjustments.

Tactical alpha: Alpha can come from tactical adjustments based on identifying mispricings that aren’t explained by factor exposures.

Portfolio Construction: the three main building blocks of portfolio construction are alpha skills, position sizing, and rewarded factor weightings.

Active return components: Comprised of exposure to rewarded factors, alpha from skill and luck.

Static factor exposure: Passive exposure to known rewarded factors is no longer seen as alpha, but timing such exposures can be.

Factor timing: Successful timing of factor exposures is considered a source of alpha.

Diversified factor approach: A manager skilled in setting factor exposures can target rewarded factors while minimizing idiosyncratic risk via diversification.

Concentrated stock picking: Stock pickers express their views through concentrated portfolios, assuming more idiosyncratic risk.

Multi-factor diversification: Multi-factor products minimize idiosyncratic risk and maintain high Active Share with low active risk via broad diversification.

Decision breadth: Confidence in a manager’s skill grows when performance comes from many independent, uncorrelated decisions.

Active risk attributable to active share: More securities or lower idiosyncratic risk reduces the portion of active risk attributed to Active Share.

Pure active share risk: If factor exposure is neutralized all active risk comes from Active Share (single-factor model).

Active share covariance: An additional security will minimize active risk contribution if it has a higher covariance as opposed to it having independent low active risk.

Scalabilty of active return: without constraints active return will increase proportionally to active weights such that the information ratio does not change.

Discretionary risk profile: The average active discretionary portfolio has higher idiosyncratic risk than a diversified multi-factor fund.

High Active Share: Concentrated portfolios often exhibit high Active Share, investors willing to pay a higher fee for greater active management.

Information ratio maximization: When using predicted active risk as the risk measure, the objective is to maximize the information ratio.

Sharpe ratio objective: If using portfolio volatility as risk, the objective shifts to maximizing the Sharpe ratio.

Sector rotator risk: A concentrated sector rotator bears significant unrewarded and idiosyncratic risks from sector and security-specific positions.

Concentration choice: Sector rotators can choose between diversified or highly concentrated portfolios, affecting exposure to unrewarded and idiosyncratic risks.

Slippage definition: Slippage is the difference between execution price and midquote at order entry, incorporating volatility and market impact.

Market-neutral strategy: Market-neutral portfolios are a form of long/short investing designed to remove systematic risk and isolate manager skill.

Long vs short drivers: Long and short sides of a market-neutral portfolio may have different economic drivers of return, requiring careful risk offset.

Reduce market noise: Market-neutral strategies aim to eliminate general market movement effects to better assess manager skill.

Skill isolation: Market-neutral strategies seek to isolate returns from manager skill in forecasting, free from broader market influences.

Systematic risk offset: Market-neutral portfolios are constructed to offset systematic risks in long positions with those in short positions.

Zero market beta: A true market-neutral portfolio targets a market beta of zero by balancing exposures on long and short sides.

Lower volatility: Market-neutral strategies tend to have lower volatility than long-only strategies and can serve as absolute return vehicles.

Diversification benefit: Market-neutral strategies often have low correlation with other strategies, providing portfolio diversification.

Short risk: If short positions move unfavorably, collateral needs rise, potentially forcing liquidations and increasing squeeze risk.

Incremental VaR: Incremental VaR (IVaR) measures how much portfolio risk changes when a new position is added.

Marginal VaR: Marginal VaR (MVaR) captures the change in portfolio risk from adjusting the size of an existing position.

4 Liability-Driven and Index-Based Strategies

Structural risk: Arises from twists and non-parallel shifts in the yield curve, reduced by minimizing bond dispersion, shifting from barbell to bullet portfolios centered on the investment horizon.

Single liability immunization: For a single liability, match Macaulay duration with the lowest convexity to the investment horizon so it resembles a zero-coupon bond.

Multiple liability immunization: To immunize multiple liabilities, assets must meet three conditions: greater convexity, equal or greater market value and BPV match with preference given to closer.

BPV selection caution: Choosing portfolios based solely on proximity of BPV to liabilities can be misleading.

ABO definition: The ABO reflects today’s accumulated legal liability using current wages and service years, discounted at a high-quality bond rate.

ABO relevance for conversion: ABO is more relevant when a plan is at risk of conversion from defined benefit to defined contribution.

PBO projection: The PBO calculates liabilities based on projected final wages, offering a forward-looking view, reported on financial statements.

PBO appropriateness: PBO is the suitable liability measure for ongoing defined benefit plan sponsors.

Interest rate sensitivity: Interest rate changes significantly impact funding status and reported earnings, especially with slim surpluses.

Zero equity duration: Equity and alternative assets are assumed to have zero duration due to unpredictability in interest rate sensitivity.

Duration gap: Asset duration minus liability duration.

Negative duration gap: Exposes pension plans to falling yields as long-dated liabilities rise; partial hedging is often used to mitigate this.

Positive duration: Benefits from falling rates: receive fixed swaps.

Model risk: Financial models face risk from faulty assumptions and inaccurate parameter estimates.

Spread risk: Treasury yields have greater volatility than corporate spreads, swaps are better as also carry credit risk.

Collateral exhaustion risk: Using derivatives in LDI strategies introduces the risk of running out of collateral.

Credit Support Annex (CSA): Eliminates counterparty risk from OTC derivatives, typically require the party with negative market value to post collateral, often cash or liquid securities.

Stratified sampling: Allows managers to optimize index performance with fewer securities and reduced tracking error, however, requiring large capital.

Fixed-income ETF arbitrage: ETF arbitrage opportunities may persist due to thin trading in underlying fixed-income securities.

Tactical allocation flexibility: Tactical asset allocation ranges give managers short-term flexibility to adjust weightings based on market outlook.

Benchmark duration alignment: Managers must align fixed-income benchmark choices with implicit or explicit duration preferences.

Duration as beta: Targeted portfolio duration reflects the investor’s preferred duration exposure and serves as the fixed-income portfolio’s beta.

Smart beta definition: Smart beta uses simple, transparent, rules-based strategies for investment decision-making.

Low-risk benchmark: For highly risk-averse investors, short-duration indexes with limited credit risk are most appropriate.

Long-duration benchmark: Investors with long horizons may prefer indexes with the longest durations.

Duration-risk trade-off: Index selection involves balancing duration exposure against risk considerations.

Cash flow yield: Immunization aims to lock in the portfolio’s internal rate of return on cash flows, not the average yield to maturity.

Swaption collar: A swaption collar combines buying a receiver (fixed) swaption and selling a payer swaption to offset premiums when negative duration gap by protecting against large rate declines.

PORTFOLIO-MANAGEMENT II

1 Yield Curve Strategies

Yield curve definition: The yield curve represents the yields-to-maturity across different maturities for a specific issuer.

Yield curve modeling: Yield curves often rely on models and interpolation, especially for constant maturity yields as many do not reflect traded securities.

Yield curve risk factors: Yield curve risk is commonly categorized by changes in level (parallel shifts), slope (twists), and curvature (butterfly movements).

Butterfly spread: The butterfly spread increases when the yield curve has more curvature, reflecting differences in intermediate-term yields.

Duration and convexity: Longer-duration bonds have higher convexity than shorter-duration ones.

Convexity value: Bonds with higher positive convexity outperform similar-duration bonds during rate changes due to favorable price behavior.

Yield curve slope and inflation: Positively sloped yield curves signal expectations of rising or stable inflation and strong economic growth.

Forecasts and yield curve: Active managers use growth and inflation forecasts to anticipate yield curve changes in level, slope, or curvature.

Stable Yield Curve: Managers must increase portfolio duration or leverage to achieve excess returns.

Repo trades: In stable rate environments, repo trades can add leveraged risk through borrowing at lower short-term rates.

Carry return equation: Carry return is calculated as (Coupon income ± Rolldown return) minus financing cost.

Rolling yield strategy: When the yield curve is upward-sloping, buying bonds with longer maturities than the investment horizon can enhance returns.

Futures strategy: Futures strategies mimic roll-down benefits but rely only on price appreciation, not coupon income.

Derivative collateral: Managers using derivatives must hold sufficient cash or collateral to meet margin requirements.

Derivative leverage: Small yield changes can significantly impact a derivative’s value due to high leverage.

Portfolio duration: A constructed portfolio can have approximately zero duration through weighted bond positions.

Slope change sensitivity: Duration and convexity don’t capture changes in slope between short- and long-term yields; specific position analysis is required.

Bear steepening: Bear steepening occurs when long-term yields rise more than short-term ones, often due to growth and inflation concerns.

Bull steepening: Bull steepening involves short-term yields falling more than long-term ones, often triggered by monetary easing during recessions.

Non-parallel shift on immunized portfolio: Asset or liability with greater convexity will dominate if money duration is matched.

Net long duration: Increasing short-term bond exposure introduces net long duration, benefiting from falling short-term yields.

Volatility strategy: Investors often prefer bonds with embedded options over stand-alone options for managing volatility.

Putable bond advantage: A putable bond gains value as yields rise, due to the right to sell it back to the issuer.

Callable bond mechanics: Callable bonds allow issuers to buy back the bond if yields fall, reducing the bond’s value to the investor.

Buying convexity: Increasing convexity can be done by buying calls, puts, or putable bonds, or by reducing exposure to MBS and callable bonds, comes at the expense of higher yield.

Volatility view: When expecting higher volatility, managers may go long volatility to benefit from large yield curve movements.

Volatility strategy tools: Managers express views on future versus implied volatility by trading bonds with embedded options or using stand-alone interest rate options.

2 Fixed-Income Active Management: Credit Strategies

One-period credit spread estimate: Can be approximated by multiplying loss given default (LGD) and probability of default (POD), most accurate for bonds trading near par.

Distressed bond pricing: As default becomes likely, distressed bonds trade based on price rather than spread, approaching the recovery rate (1 − LGD).

Credit migration impact: Credit rating changes usually negatively affect bond prices, as downgrades are more likely and have greater spread impacts than upgrades.

Investment grade bonds: More at risk of credit rating migration, credit spread volatility is a more relevant consideration than loss severity. Spread duration measures the credit spread volatility risk.

Upward-sloping credit curve: An upward-sloping credit spread curve signals low near-term default risk that increases over time.

Credit spread inversion: Short-term spreads become wider than long-term spread during economic downturn for high-yield issuers as the probability for downgrade is higher.

Lower-rated issuers and credit cycle: Lower-rated issuers show larger credit spread curve shifts and higher default losses during downturns.

High-yield spreads vs. government yields: Under stress, high-yield credit spreads widen while government yields fall, creating a negative correlation.

Yield curve mismatch bias: Spread comparisons between bonds with different maturities introduce measurement bias when the yield curve is sloped.

Yield spread definition: Yield spread is the difference between a bond’s YTM and a similar-maturity government bond’s YTM.

G-spread: The difference between bond YTM and a linear interpolation of on-the-run government bond YTMs.

I-spread: The difference between a bond’s YTM and a linear interpolation of MRR swap rates where the swap rate is derived as the government YTM + swap spread

Asset swap spread (ASW): The difference between a bond’s coupon and the MRR swap rates achieved by converting fixed coupons to floating via an asset swap, priced mark-to-market.

Z-spread: The constant yield spread applied at each maturity, more accurate and complex.

Negative CDS basis: Negative basis occurs when a bond’s yield spread exceeds its CDS spread.

CDS upfront payment: protection buyer pays contract value CDS swap rate above coupon rate (1% IG, 5% high-yield) multiplied by the effective spread duration of notional value.

OAS definition: Similar to the Z-spread it is the constant spread over one-period forward rates that makes the arbitrage-free value of the bond equal to its market price using assumed volatility.

Default-adjusted return: Expected excess return accounts for both default probability and loss severity.

Sovereign credit analysis: For sovereigns, creditworthiness depends on economic activity and the government’s tax capacity and willingness.

Structural credit model: Structural models define default risk as the chance that an issuer’s asset value falls below its liabilities.

Reduced form model: Reduced form models estimate POD using observable financial and macroeconomic variables.

Equity volatility and default: Higher equity volatility increases the chance of an issuer’s assets falling below the default threshold.

Spread and YTM correlation assumption: Analytical duration assumes no correlation between government YTMs and credit spreads, which may not hold in practice.

Credit rating lag: Public credit ratings often lag market pricing; active managers prefer spread-based credit quality measures like OAS.

Rating focus differences: S&P ratings emphasize POD, while Moody’s focus on expected credit losses.

Wide spreads and spread changes: Bonds with wider spreads tend to exhibit larger spread changes, affecting portfolio credit risk metrics.

Emerging market debt concentration: Emerging and frontier markets are often dominated by sovereign issuers in concentrated domestic industries such as banking and commodities.

Covered bonds vs ABS: Covered bonds, real estate exposure, have lower credit risk and yields than ABS due to dual recourse and substitution of non-performing assets.

CLOs vs. CDOs: CLO tranches benefit more from short-term rate increases than CDOs, due to their leveraged loan exposure tied to MRRs plus spread.

Parametric method limitation: Parametric methods are unsuitable for non-normal or option-based portfolios, unlike historical simulation.

CDX index protection: Selling protection on CDX is a long credit spread risk position; buying protection on a CDX subindex creates selective exposure.

Tranche correlation: As default correlations increase, the value of mezzanine tranches usually increases relative to the value of senior tranches.

3 Trade Strategy and Execution

Execution risk: Adverse price movement risk from both fundamental value changes and trading activity.

Decision price: The decision price is the security price at the time the manager chose to trade, used as the benchmark price for managers seeking short-term alpha.

Alpha decay: Short-term alpha loss after the decision is made.

Close price benchmark: Used by managers targeting tracking error.

Previous close benchmark: Quantitative managers often use the previous close as a proxy for the decision price in models and optimizers.

Opening price benchmark: Using the opening price as a benchmark can be inappropriate if the trade occurs in the opening auction, as the trade may influence the benchmark.

TWAP benchmark: An equal-weighted average price over the trading period, used when volume is uncertain or large trades at extreme prices may distort benchmarks that participants cannot access.

Broker risk/principal trade: A sell-side broker crosses an order with their own book, used when a sell-side broker cannot match client orders elsewhere.

High touch approach: For illiquid securities the broker has to exercise judgment between their option at hand.

Agency trades: In difficult markets, rather than taking positions themselves, dealers will act as agents/brokers to find counterparties for the client.

VWAP and TWAP algorithms: A scheduled strategy whereby algorithms trade a set number of shares on a time-based schedule, not adapting to liquidity changes. Risk of not completing the order.

Participation algorithms (POV): A scheduled strategy that follow real time volumes, may incur higher trading costs by continuing to buy as prices move higher and to sell as prices move lower.

Scheduled algorithm suitability: Scheduled algorithms are used when managers expect stable prices and prefer minimal market impact over speed.

Liquidity-seeking algorithms: Trade aggressively when favorable liquidity appears, often using multiple venues or dark pools for large trades to avoid market leakage.

Dark aggregator algorithms: Avoid lit markets to reduce information leakage, trading in opaque venues when size and market impact are concerns.

Arrival price algorithms: Used when managers expect price movement and wish to trade aggressively to capture mispricing/alpha or reduce risk.

Smart order routers: SORs route orders to venues with the highest execution probability and best price using real-time data.

Small order execution: SORs are used to execute small orders as either marketable or non-marketable orders.

Cash drag minimization: Equitize uninvested cash in futures or ETFs and gradually invest in desired investments.

Emerging markets and dark pools: In emerging markets, dark pool trading remains minimal compared to traditional exchanges.

OTC liquidity: Liquidity has decreased for OTC instruments not suited to central clearing or public trade reporting.

Paper return: The paper return reflects the hypothetical return if the trade occurred at the decision price without costs.

Opportunity cost: Opportunity cost reflects the impact of unexecuted shares and missed execution at the decision price.

Arrival price benchmark: Measures the difference between the market price at order release and the actual transaction price.

TWAP benchmark use: TWAP helps evaluate whether fair prices were achieved over time, excluding outliers.

Best execution: Best execution balances price, cost, and other objectives, not just the lowest cost.

Pro-rata allocation: Trades should be allocated pro-rata across accounts to ensure fairness and transparency.

OTC transparency: Traditionally lacked transparency in prices, trade sizes, and structure. Introduction of central clearing facilities has resulted in contract standardization and price discovery.
