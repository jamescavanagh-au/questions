Question 3: The instrument recommended by Zame to adjust the interest rate risk of Client 2’s pension plan will most likely increase the plan's:

spread risk.

Correct because the futures contracts recommended by Zame are on US Treasuries, whereas the plan’s liabilities are corporate obligations. Movements in the corporate/Treasury yield spread introduce risk to the hedging strategy. Usually, yields on high-quality corporate bonds are less volatile than on more-liquid Treasuries. Government bonds are used in a wide variety of hedging as well as speculative trading strategies by institutional investors. Also, inflows of international funds typically are placed in government bonds, at least until they are allocated to other asset classes. Those factors lead to greater volatility in Treasury yields than comparable-maturity corporate bonds.

asset liquidity risk.

Incorrect because US Treasury futures contracts are one of the most liquid investments in the financial markets and thus would not have any particular asset liquidity risk when they are used in managing the interest rate gap for the plan. Asset liquidity is a risk factor in strategies that combine active investing with otherwise passive fixed-income portfolios. This risk is particularly important in contingent immunization.

counterparty credit risk.

Incorrect because futures contracts are exchange traded and as a result have no counterparty credit risk.

TOPIC: PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION

Gert Stein chairs DT Bank's asset-liability management committee. The committee is aware that DT's board of directors wants to improve the company's competitive position and stock price. The committee discusses DT's capital structure and Stein notes the following:

  • The equity capital ratio is 12%
  • The modified duration of assets is 2.0
  • The modified duration of liabilities is 1.3
  • The yield on liabilities is expected to move by 70 bps for every 100 bps of yield change in the asset portfolio

Currently the yield curve is upward sloping. Stein expects the yield curve to undergo a gradual 200 bps parallel upward shift over the next 18 months. To take advantage of current low interest rates, Stein proposes issuing fixed-rate bonds maturing in 15 years and investing the proceeds in highly rated short-term commercial paper.

The committee has decided to reduce the bank's holdings of long-term fixed-rate sovereign debt securities and to increase commercial lending. The new loans would be floating-rate, investment grade, and have long-term maturities.

Question 5: Calculate the percentage change in the value of DT Bank's equity capital from an immediate 50 bps increase in the level of yields on its asset portfolio.

General Feedback

The following equation is used to calculate the modified duration of shareholder equity capital:

DE = (A/E) DA – (A/E – 1) DL (Δi/Δy)

where DE, DA, and DL are modified duration of shareholder equity, assets, and liabilities, respectively; A is assets, L is liabilities, and E is shareholder equity.

Δi/Δy is the change in yield on liabilities for a given change in yield on assets.

A/E = 1/0.12 = 8.333; (A/E – 1) =7.333; DA = 2.0; DL = 1.3; and Δi/Δy = 0.70

DE = (A/E) DA – (A/E – 1) DL (Δi/Δy)

DE = (8.333 × 2) – (7.333 × 1.3) × 0.70 = 10.0

To determine the effect of a 50 basis point increase in portfolio asset yields, we multiply the yield change by the duration of equity capital:

0.50% × –10.0 = –5.0%

Question 6: Explain , given Stein's expectations, why his proposed strategy will most likely lead to:

i. a decrease in short-term earnings.

ii. an increase in long-term earnings.

Note: Each explanation should be in a separate paragraph.

General Feedback

Stein's 's proposal would likely cause earnings to decline in the short-term, because of the negative spread between the new longer-term debt and short-term commercial paper. The yield curve is upward sloping.

This effect should be temporary, however, if Stein's forecast of a 200 bps parallel rise in rates proves correct. DT would be positioned to take advantage of higher-yielding investments over time, funded with long-term borrowing at below-market interest rates. The lower-yielding commercial paper will mature at some point over the next 18 months, and the 15-year maturity of the debt would give DT flexibility to invest in high-quality commercial paper at higher rates which allows the trade to be additive to earnings.

Question 7: Determine the most likely effect on DT Bank's interest rate risk of the committee's decision to shift investments to commercial lending from sovereign debt.

(Decrease, Increase)

Justify your response.

General Feedback

Interest rate risk would most likely decline with a switch from fixed-rate to floating-rate assets. The floating rates on the commercial loans would be reset periodically, usually every 3 to 6 months. Interest earnings on the loans would track market interest rate movements, and asset values would stay near par with minimal or no exposure to changing rates. Therefore, the shift to floating-rate assets will result in a decrease in interest rate risk.

Question 12: Discuss two reasons that support Anwauh's use of a returns-based style analysis for Gaia.

Note: Each discussion should be in a separate paragraph.

General Feedback

A returns-based analysis is likely to objectively measure the portfolio's sensitivity and underlying risk characteristics. The usefulness of holdings-based analysis may be compromised because the portfolio reflects a snapshot in time and might not reflect the portfolio going forward, particularly for high-turnover strategies. Gaia's strategy has high portfolio turnover, which makes holdings-based style analysis less effective.

Gaia reports monthly returns and the returns of a style-based benchmark. Since returns are provided by Gaia, their style and important return drivers can be analyzed in the context of a returns-based style analysis. A returns-based approach analyzes a manager's investment style by comparing the returns of the employed strategy to those of a set of style indexes. RBSA should identify the important drivers of return and risk factors for the period analyzed and can be estimated even for complicated strategies. In addition, the process is comparable across managers and through time, and the use of returns data provides an objective style check that is not subject to window dressing. The analysis can be run immediately after the data is available, particularly in the case of publicly traded securities.

Question 13: Discuss three reasons that support the Minerva portfolio manager's claim that she follows a quantitative investment strategy.

Note: Each discussion should be in a separate paragraph.

General Feedback

Minerva's strategy is consistent with a quantitative strategy.  Minerva looks at firm characteristics that correlate with economic macro-level factors and constructs factor exposures from these characteristics, which it uses to invest across a large number of firms. The factors are back-tested and statistically validated. Stocks in the portfolio are traded at predetermined levels when certain thresholds are met (systematic rebalancing). These are all characteristics of a quantitative strategy.

In addition, a large proportion of return is explained by factor exposures (88% = 9.57% / 10.90%) and it has a low alpha (0.13% = 10.90% – 1.2% – 9.57%). Both of these indicate low unexplained return (idiosyncratic risk) in the portfolio.

General Feedback

The following are five criteria that will help Bonnela in effectively specifying asset classes for the purpose of constructing Client 1’s strategic asset allocation:

  • Assets within an asset class should be relatively homogeneous. Assets within an asset class should have similar attributes. For example, defining equities to include both real estate and common stock would result in a non-homogeneous asset class.
  • Asset classes should be mutually exclusive. Overlapping asset classes will reduce the effectiveness of strategic asset allocation in controlling risk and could introduce problems in developing asset class return expectations. For example, if one asset class for a US investor is domestic common equities, then world equities ex-US is more appropriate as another asset class rather than global equities, which include US equities.
  • Asset classes should be diversifying. For risk control purposes, an included asset class should not have extremely high expected correlations with other asset classes or with a linear combination of other asset classes. Otherwise, the included asset class will be effectively redundant in a portfolio because it will duplicate risk exposures already present. In general, a pairwise correlation above 0.95 is undesirable (given a sufficient number of observations to have confidence in the correlation estimate).
  • The asset classes as a group should make up a preponderance of world investable wealth. From the perspective of portfolio theory, selecting an asset allocation from a group of asset classes satisfying this criterion should tend to increase expected return for a given level of risk. Furthermore, the inclusion of more markets expands the opportunities for applying active investment strategies, assuming the decision to invest actively has been made. However, such factors as regulatory restrictions on investments and government-imposed limitations on investment by foreigners may limit the asset classes an investor can invest in.
  • Asset classes selected for investment should have the capacity to absorb a meaningful proportion of an investor’s portfolio. Liquidity and transaction costs are both significant considerations. If liquidity and expected transaction costs for an investment of a size meaningful for an investor are unfavorable, an asset class may not be practically suitable for investment.

General Feedback

The credit risk of a non-deliverable forward (NDF) is typically lower than for the outright forward because the principal sums in the NDF do not move, unlike an outright “vanilla” forward contract.
