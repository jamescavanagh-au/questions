Estella Weniger is a private wealth manager serving high-net-worth individuals. She is meeting with one of her clients, Alfred Abcott.

Abcott wants to invest $10 million in equities. Weniger discusses with Abcott whether an actively managed portfolio or a passive index-based fund is more suitable for his investment. During the discussion, Abcott highlights his beliefs and preferences:

  • He is cost conscious
  • He does not believe equity markets are efficient
  • He has very specific exclusion restrictions due to his religious and cultural beliefs
  • He wants to avoid "key person" risk

After considering Abcott's beliefs and preferences, Weniger suggests an actively managed equity portfolio.

Weniger forecasts a nominal return of 8% per year and that inflation will be 2% per year. Weniger calculates the expected after-tax inflation-adjusted accumulation for Abcott's portfolio.

Question 1: Explain , with two reasons, why the actively managed approach is more suitable for Abcott.

Note: Each explanation should be in a separate paragraph.

General Feedback

Being critical of the efficient market hypothesis supports the choice of active management. The absence of market efficiency opens up the potential for successful active management. The efficient market hypothesis gave credence to investors' interest in indexes by theorizing that stock prices incorporate all relevant information – implying that after costs, the majority of active investors could not consistently outperform the market.

The presence of very specific exclusion restrictions also points towards active management, since it is difficult to identify a suitable index. Client-specific investment mandates, such as those related to ESG considerations, are typically managed actively. This active approach occurs because index-based management may not be particularly efficient or cost effective when managers must meet a client's desired holdings (or holdings to avoid).

The other two points highlighted by Abcott do not support the choice of active management. Instead, the comparatively low costs and the avoidance of "key person" risk are advantages of an index-based approach. Consequently, index-based management fees are generally much lower than fees charged by active managers. This fee differential represents the most significant and enduring advantage of index-based management. Additionally, "key person" risk is relevant for active managers if the success of an investment manager's firm is dependent on one or a few individuals ("star managers") who may potentially leave the firm.

Question 3: Identify the most appropriate rebalancing strategy for Abcott's portfolio based on Weniger's market expectations.

(Time-based rebalancing, Threshold-based rebalancing)

Discuss one disadvantage of the identified strategy.

General Feedback

Threshold-based rebalancing reacts to changing market conditions, which means that it is most responsive in a market environment of high volatility. Time-based rebalancing meanwhile is not very responsive to changing market conditions, due to the strict definition of rebalancing intervals. Threshold-based rebalancing reacts to changing market conditions: the portfolio is rebalanced whenever there is a significant deviation from its target allocation, that is, whenever upper or lower rebalancing limits are breached. This strategy is sensitive to market volatility and sudden shifts. However, the time-based rebalancing strategy is somewhat unresponsive to significant and sudden market changes, which may expose the portfolio to higher risks or cause missed investment opportunities between scheduled rebalancing times.

Threshold-based rebalancing is inherently complex, often requiring more sophisticated monitoring and automation systems to keep track of portfolio drift and execute rebalancing when needed.

Because the rebalancing trigger is tied closely to market fluctuations, threshold-based rebalancing has the risk of overreacting to short-term market events, potentially skewing away from long-term investment goals.

Finally, the timing of threshold-based rebalancing can be inconsistent. During stable markets, there may not be an adjustment for long periods. However, during periods of volatility, there may be frequent rebalancing in response to sudden periods of persistent volatility, which can create a more short-term focus.

Question 4: Determine which country has greater wealth inequality.

(Country A, Country B)

Justify your response.

General Feedback

Wealth inequality is measured with the help of the Gini coefficient. The higher the Gini coefficient, the greater is the wealth inequality in the country. Country B at 0.72 has a much higher Gini coefficient than Country A at 0.39 and thus greater wealth inequality.

The Gini coefficient is a broad-based measure of inequality that focuses on the entirety of the wealth spectrum. It ranges from 0 to 1, where 0 represents perfect equality (everybody gets an equal share of the wealth) and 1 represents perfect inequality (one individual owns all the wealth).

Question 9: Determine which bond considered by Becker would most likely increase in value if his interest rate volatility forecast is realized.

(Callable bond, Putable bond)

Justify your response.

General Feedback

Interest rate call and put values are positively related to interest rate volatility.

The value of a callable bond is the value of an otherwise identical option-free bond minus the value of an interest rate call option. A decrease in volatility decreases the value of the call and therefore increases the value of the callable bond.

The value of a putable bond is the value of an otherwise identical option-free bond plus the value of an interest rate put option. A decrease in volatility decreases the value of the put and therefore decreases the value of the putable bond.

Question 10: Determine which of the following is the best positioning strategy for Jenkins given her expectation of a flattening in the Treasury yield curve.

(Strategy 1, Strategy 2, Strategy 3)

Justify your response with one reason for each component of the selected strategy.

Note: Each justification should be in a separate paragraph.

General Feedback

Given her expectation that 30-year yields will decline and 2-year-yields will rise, Jenkins needs to be long duration exposure to 30-year yields and short duration exposure to 2-year yields. The long position in the 30-year swaption in Strategy 1 gives the right to receive fixed on a 30-year swap, which will increase in value if 30-year rates fall. The long position in the 2-year bond put option will increase in value if short-term rates rise, causing short-term bond prices to fall.

Under Strategy 2, the right to pay fixed on a 30-year swap has been sold. If 30-year rates fall, the value of the swaption decreases, which is positive for the swaption seller. However, the long position in the 2-year bond call option loses value if short-term rates rise as part of a curve flattening.

Under Strategy 3, the right to receive fixed on a 30-year swap has been sold. If 30-year rates fall as part of a curve flattening, the value of the swaption increases which is unfavorable to the swaption seller. The long position in the 2-year bond put option will increase in value, per the discussion above.

Therefore, only Strategy 1 entails profitable positions at both ends of the yield curve under the curve flattening expected by Jenkins.

Question 11: Determine which of the following yield curve changes would result in the best relative performance for the Euro government bond portfolio.

(Upward parallel shift, Increase in the butterfly spread)

Justify your response.

General Feedback

The butterfly spread is equal to (2 × medium-term yield) – (short-term yield + long-term yield). An increase in the spread implies that short-term and long-term term yields decline relative to the medium-term yield.

The appropriate positioning in anticipation of an increase in the butterfly spread is to be long bonds at the short and long ends of the yield curve and short bonds at the intermediate point on the yield curve. From the active key rate durations in Exhibit 1, this is Jenkins's portfolio positioning relative to his benchmark.

Upward parallel shift is not correct, as the Euro government bond portfolio and the index will be affected similarly by the yield curve shift (the sum of the KRDs is 10.04 for the portfolio and 10.05 for the index).

Question 17: Explain why using variance swaps to hedge against increased volatility could result in a higher payoff than using VIX futures.

General Feedback

The main causes of payoff differences between variance swaps and VIX futures are due to convexity, the measure of volatility used to calculate the payout, and the structure of the VIX futures curve.

Variance swaps have a valuable convexity feature – as realized volatility increases, the positive payoff on the swap increases. This convexity occurs because a long variance swap is equivalent to being long a basket of options and short the underlying asset. Hence, a long position in a variance swap is long gamma and has a convex payoff.

Variance swaps are instruments used to take directional bets on implied versus realized volatility, whereas VIX futures allow investors to implement views on expectations about the timing and magnitude of a change in implied volatility. For a variance swap, the mark-to-market valuation and payout is the difference between the fixed variance strike agreed in the contract (implied variance) and the realized variance on the underlying over the period specified. For a long VIX futures position, the payout is determined by the change in market implied volatility. To protect against a downturn, the market’s implied volatility must increase by more than the consensus expectation of implied volatility prior to the downturn. Consequently, since Reddington expects realized volatility will increase more than market implied volatility, the payoff on variance swaps should be higher than that of the VIX futures.

Establishing long positions in VIX futures can be expensive over time. Since the VIX futures curve is upward sloping, the carrying costs created from the contract roll down are elevated (high carry cost).

However, as long as there is a willing counterparty, a swap can be initiated on virtually any index.

This swap is an OTC contract for a given period with a counterparty, such as a bank, to exchange cash payments at a fixed settlement frequency. The client account receives the index return exactly; in exchange, the account pays the counterparty a standard (typically floating) market reference rate plus an agreed-upon management fee. This approach requires no trading by PAM and little additional information other than the return of the index.
