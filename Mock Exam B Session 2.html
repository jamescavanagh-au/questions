Question 7: Discuss how the Endowment's liquidity risk would be affected by each of the following:

i. The change in spending policy
ii. The capital campaign

Note: Each discussion should be in a separate paragraph.

General Feedback

An endowment's liabilities are the future stream of payouts to the university which are typically codified in the spending policy. One of the purposes of the spending policy is to smooth endowment payouts to partially insulate contributions to the university from capital markets volatility – known as liquidity risk.

The endowment had been run using the constant growth rule, which the board recently changed to the hybrid rule. Under the constant growth rule an endowment typically provides a fixed amount annually to the university adjusted for inflation. During periods of weak average returns the spending amount expressed as a percentage of assets may become very high, increasing liquidity risk.

The hybrid rule uses fixed weights on the two different spending policies: constant growth and market value. The market value rule involves the endowment paying a pre-specified percentage of the moving average of asset values. This reduces liquidity risk, because as markets experience periods of weak average returns, the amount paid out also decreases. By incorporating the market value rule as a percentage of the payout, the liquidity risk is decreased.

Universities regularly raise money from donors. Because of gifts and donations, endowments' net spending rate tends to be lower than the headline spending rates. On average, the net spend is lower and the donations and gifts take the place of the funds that would normally be paid out by the endowment. The university has recently been building up its alumni outreach and has started a successful fund-raising campaign. The funds raised in this campaign would decrease the endowment's net spend and therefore, liquidity risk.

Note: Each explanation should be in a separate paragraph.

General Feedback

The GIPS standards require all actual, fee-paying, discretionary segregated accounts to be included in at least one composite. All actual, fee-paying, discretionary pooled funds must also be included in at least one composite if they meet a composite definition. In this case, the pooled fund must be included in the fixed-income composite and the pension fund account must be excluded. The charity account may be included, but is not required to be included, in the composite.

The pooled fund must be included because pooled funds must be included in any composite for which they meet the composite definition.

Ruttan may, but is not required to, include the charity account in the composite. However, including the charity account (a non-fee-paying account) in the composite may require Ruttan to make additional disclosures. Discretionary segregated accounts that are non-fee-paying may be included in composites, but neither non-discretionary nor simulated or model portfolios may be included in any composite.

Ruttan must exclude the pension fund account from the composite. The pension fund account meets the definition of non-discretionary since it places restrictions on the manager's ability to execute derivatives transactions, which are used to manage interest rate risk – a key risk in a fixed-income strategy. If a client imposes restrictions on a manager’s freedom to make investment decisions to buy, hold, and sell securities so as to carry out the investment strategy and achieve the portfolio’s financial objectives, then the manager must consider whether the portfolio is in fact discretionary. In general, restrictions that impede the investment process to such an extent that the strategy cannot be implemented as intended may be presumed to render the portfolio non-discretionary, and it should not be included in a composite.

General Feedback

To be able to link historical composite performance to the composites' performance after an acquisition, four criteria must be met under the GIPS standards:

1. Substantially all the investment decision makers are employed by the new or acquiring firm.

2. The decision-making process remains substantially intact and independent within the new or acquiring firm.

3. The new or acquiring firm has records that document and support the reported performance.

4. There must be no break in the track record between the past firm or affiliation and the new or acquiring firm.

All four criteria would be met with respect to Firm A, so the historical performance of Firm A's composites may be linked to the composites' post-acquisition performance. The 1st criterion would be met because four of Firm A's five portfolio managers (investment decision makers) would remain after the acquisition. The 2nd criterion would be met because Firm A would continue to operate with its own management structure and would not require approval from Ruttan for investment strategy changes. The vignette states that the 3rd and 4th criteria would be met.

Only two of the criteria would be met with respect to Firm B: #3 and #4, as stated in the vignette. The 1st criterion would not be met, as half of Firm B's portfolio managers would not remain after the acquisition. Also, although Firm B would continue to operate with its own management structure, the 2nd criterion would not be met because any changes to Firm B's investment strategy would require approval from Ruttan.
